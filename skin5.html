Algorithmic Bias in AI-Assisted Skin Disease Detection: A Review of Causes, Impacts, and Mitigation Strategies

Dr. Nitin Mishra1
1Professor, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Parul University, Vadodara, India
Corresponding author: drnitinmishra10@gmail.com
Abstract
Algorithmic bias in artificial intelligence (AI) systems for skin disease detection represents a critical challenge in healthcare technology. This paper examines how demographic imbalances in training datasets—particularly the underrepresentation of darker skin tones—lead to systematic disparities in diagnostic accuracy. We review the causes of algorithmic bias, including data scarcity and quality issues, analyze its far-reaching impacts on healthcare equity, and evaluate proposed mitigation strategies such as diverse dataset curation, algorithmic fairness techniques, continuous auditing, and explainable AI. Furthermore, we discuss the ethical responsibilities of developers and clinicians, regulatory frameworks, and the need for interdisciplinary collaboration. Our analysis underscores that addressing algorithmic bias is essential to ensuring equitable healthcare outcomes and maintaining public trust in AI-driven diagnostic tools.
Keywords: algorithmic bias, artificial intelligence, dermatology, skin disease detection, healthcare equity, machine learning fairness, explainable AI, medical imaging

I. Introduction

Algorithmic bias, or systematic unfair discrimination in decision-making processes facilitated by algorithmic systems, emerges when machines are programmed by processes and trained with datasets of insufficient quality. As a result, algorithmic systems are prone to favor certain groups of people over others. In health technology, and more specifically in dermatology, skin diseases are diagnosed based on images of skin conditions manifest on the skin's surface. Thus, in AI-assisted skin disease detection, biased algorithms can lead to incorrect and unequal healthcare and recommendations for treatment. These problems affect in particular underrepresented populations [1].

AI technologies have recently exploded into the field of dermatology, promising higher levels of accuracy, speed, and accessibility to the diagnosis and treatment of skin disease. Currently, numerous systems are able to identify a wide variety of skin conditions, both common and rare, and even on occasion diagnose instances of skin cancer such as melanoma [8]. High-performing deep learning systems are able to process images at an unprecedented rate, enabling them to efficiently analyze dermatological images to provide speedy diagnosis, reducing the burden on healthcare professionals who can be delayed for long periods waiting for results from laboratory tests and samples. However, with the exciting potential that there is for AI systems in the dermatological field, there are also many challenges facing this technology, such as addressing and eradicating issues of bias found within the algorithms used in decision-making in AI algorithmic systems [13].

II. Demographic Imbalance in Training Datasets

A. Data Scarcity and Representation Gaps

The issue of demographic imbalances within a training dataset has become more apparent within the last decade. Since the majority of images used for AI-model training come from affluent individuals of light skin color, the rate of errors within skin cancer image classification by AI models is likely to be greater for individuals of darker skin color. A study has revealed how melanoma images of individuals of darker skin tones have been greatly underrepresented within training datasets for deep-learning-based image classification models for dermatological image analysis, which are likely to lead to further health disparities through the misdiagnosis of conditions that are already observed to present differently across various skin types [8]. Specifically, individuals with a darker skin tone are at greater risk of melanoma being misdiagnosed through the reliance on AI models, as previously stated regarding how melanoma can present differently upon individuals of various skin types [1]. This is particularly problematic, given the propensity for such conditions to be often diagnosed at a later stage within darker-skinned individuals.

A further dimension to the problem of data scarcity for training AI health systems relates to the quality of the available data and whether it is representative of the population that the system is intended to support. The majority of datasets used to train AI systems for dermatological image analysis do not contain a sufficient number of images of different skin types or of a variety of different skin conditions [21]. Therefore, models are not able to generalize to the particular presentation of a condition on different skin types, resulting in poor performance that is often reported as a high variance in performance metrics. The limited ability of models to learn the differences in presentation of skin conditions between different ethnic groups further exacerbates the trend of bias in health technology.

B. Performance Disparities Across Skin Tones

Differential performance of currently available skin analysis models on different skin tones has highlighted issues in the differential provision of dermatological care to individuals of different ethnic groups. Studies have shown that most current models are trained on images of light-skinned individuals and, as such, have poor performance in individuals with darker skin tones. These disparities highlight a critical issue in healthcare and provide further evidence of existing bias in the provision of care to individuals of different ethnic groups [9].

III. Systemic Impact on Healthcare Equity

A. Clinical Consequences of Misdiagnosis

The impact of algorithmic bias in health technology extends beyond individual cases of error to become a systemic issue with far-reaching implications for healthcare equity. As more clinical decisions are informed by AI, the potential for these systems to lock in and even amplify existing disparities threatens to undermine the potential of health technology to improve the lives of all people. In order to avoid reinforcing inequity and instead support improved health outcomes for individuals and populations around the world, it is critical that the development and deployment of health technology is treated as a global health issue, with all of the corresponding complexity and challenges that this designation implies.

Consequences of such errors lead to further harm of patients. Misdiagnosed skin conditions can be treated incorrectly with adverse effects or even worsen the initial condition. The errors can cause individuals to lose faith in the healthcare system, especially from underrepresented populations who are already disenfranchised from healthcare. Such errors also can lead to delayed treatment of life-threatening skin conditions. Skin cancer, for example, can spread rapidly if left untreated. Thus, it is crucial that healthcare providers make accurate diagnoses to ensure that their patients receive the best possible treatment.

By the same token, there is potential for additional and different stigma to be heaped upon the very same groups of people who already experience a disproportionate burden of skin diseases for which AI systems are unable to accurately predict or diagnose. When these diseases are ignored or even minimized by AI systems, they are similarly ignored and minimized in the discourse of the clinical setting, in publications, and in research agendas. The end result is that there is a lack of money, time, and resources devoted to the kind of research and development that is so desperately needed to truly provide all individuals with access to and receipt of high-quality healthcare.

B. Economic and Social Implications

There are also economic consequences to algorithmic bias. For instance, if a doctor uses an AI to recommend a treatment for a patient's skin condition and the AI recommends a less expensive treatment, then that could affect the patient's insurance coverage and overall cost of care. For instance, studies have found that people of color are less likely to receive treatment for skin cancer than Caucasians, and that this could have economic consequences as well as health consequences. For instance, untreated skin cancer can lead to serious health problems and even death. Thus, the under-treatment of skin cancer found in people of color not only affects their health but also could increase public health costs in the long run [3].

The socio-ethical implications of enabling algorithmic bias in skin disease detection have far-reaching effects and have the potential to create negative stigmas in already underrepresented demographics. As more information regarding the bias in currently available algorithms surfaces, public outcry for the need for more accountable and transparent design will continue to grow. The implementation of health IT requires the continued trust of the healthcare system's stakeholders, and as such, the negative bias in currently available algorithms for skin disease detection can serve as an example of the many socio-ethical challenges that must be addressed in the design of new health IT technologies.

IV. Mitigation Strategies and Solutions

A. Dataset Diversification and Quality Improvement

To address algorithmic bias in health technologies, solutions that are in place to tackle bias in other applications can be utilized. Firstly, the training data used to train models for health applications must be of the highest standard and more representative of a variety of populations, taking into consideration the diversity of human skin. Secondly, there are a number of algorithmic fairness techniques and methods that have been designed to identify and to correct for bias in models and to audit their decision systems for unfairness. These techniques can be implemented as part of the model development process to help to design healthier and fairer systems. Finally, a number of techniques are being designed and implemented for continuous auditing and for the evaluation of health technologies for bias and for their fairness when applied to different populations over time.

The challenge of addressing bias in AI-driven health technologies for detection of skin diseases needs to be framed in the broader challenge of creating fair health technologies and achieving equity in healthcare. A large body of evidence regarding the causes, impact, and ways to address algorithmic bias has already been developed for a number of sectors and areas where AI algorithmic decision-making already takes place, or is increasingly being considered. For AI-driven health technologies developed and used for detection and diagnosis of skin diseases, there is a particular need to recognize the current difficulties in developing models that perform well on a wide range of individuals, and to build on existing research that investigates and addresses bias. In particular, a focus on the challenges and difficulties, as well as successes, of using datasets, and of applying existing techniques for audit, evaluation, fairness, and accountability, within dermatology and for applications of AI in dermatology, is warranted.

A solution to the problem of population-specific biases embedded in the majority of existing datasets—inevitably underperforming in minority populations—would be a more diverse dataset (of highest quality) in order to ensure that the AI system can recognize all forms of skin diseases (and their subforms), which occur in all skin types and in all ethnic groups. In the case of lacking adequate, well-curated datasets, it is essential to create new datasets in clinics which include large numbers of patients of very many different ethnic groups, in very many different age groups, and of all different skin types [20].

B. Algorithmic Fairness Techniques

Interventions could include creating more representative datasets that encompass a wider array of skin types, as emphasized by researchers advocating for more inclusive data sourcing practices. Furthermore, Liu et al. [16] propose employing algorithmic fairness techniques and employing "adversarial training" to ensure that AI systems learn equally from variations in skin types rather than predominantly from a single demographic. Nonetheless, a multi-faceted approach is required, incorporating stakeholder engagement from healthcare professionals and affected communities to develop strategies that address not only technical vulnerabilities but also broader social determinants of healthcare equity.

In an attempt to establish a normative model, there has been increasing interest in frameworks relating to Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency in Machine Learning, referred to by the abbreviation FAT/ML [22]. Implementing an approach that takes account of all possible factors influencing health disparities in a given society within an algorithm so that individuals are dealt with in a fashion that is to a large extent to be considered as being fair is among the chief goals set by these frameworks for designers of such models and their application. Consequently, every designer of models for healthcare, by implementing the principles established by FAT/ML, has to make great efforts to deal with issues which would otherwise be hidden.

C. Explainable AI and Transparency

The use of techniques for explainable AI (XAI) may also be helpful for identifying the patterns in data which produce bias in AI models. Techniques for XAI aim to enable a user to provide an explanation of a decision made by a computer for a given input. The model that produces the output and the explanations of the decisions made by the model for the given input enable users of the system to understand why the system made a particular decision, and, indeed, to provide confidence in the decisions made by the model. XAI is of particular value in clinical decision-making where accurate and correct decision-making has potentially critical consequences for the patient being treated. XAI can also help identify and mitigate possible sources of AI-assisted error. For example, when a computer diagnoses a patient with AI assistance, XAI can also produce a number of alternative diagnoses and provide supporting justification for each, in turn, that the clinician can use in order to form an informed opinion, and to decide upon the best course of action, on the basis of the available information [10].

Transparency of AI algorithms, their decision processes, and their evaluation criteria is also critical for assessing the fairness of a model. There is a strong argument that all AI systems should be developed within an open framework, where the architecture of the system is clearly laid out in a published framework. Within such a framework, the criteria used for evaluation of decision-making within the AI system should also be laid out in sufficient detail. This would allow end-users to understand the basis on which decisions are made and provide a basis for assessing the fairness of the system [10].

V. Empirical Evidence of Algorithmic Bias

A. Performance Evaluation Studies

This phenomenon can be further investigated by employing a range of methods including an audit of an existing system, and/or an evaluation of the performance of a newly developed system, for fairness. A study conducted by Daneshjou et al. [5] supported the notion that underrepresentation of data from a number of different demographic groups leads to not only suboptimal performance of a number of different AI models but also can exacerbate current health inequities. In the context of the current study, this is particularly salient as there are a number of existing AI systems that are currently used for a number of different dermatological applications and have been designed using a number of different biased models. These models have been designed and trained using images of a number of different skin types, but have not been optimized for the detection and diagnosis of a number of different skin conditions in individuals of color.

A number of studies have reported on the challenges faced by a number of different dermatologists when using current AI systems for a number of different clinical applications [15]. These studies have reported a number of instances in which a number of different common skin conditions have been missed or misdiagnosed in patients of color using current AI systems. This can create a number of negative consequences including providing suboptimal treatment for a number of different patients, and potentially creating negative perceptions of current AI technology among a number of different healthcare providers.

B. Case Studies and Audit Findings

Similar findings were reported by Kakish et al. [15] when they examined the performance of a commercially available AI for the diagnosis of skin conditions. The tool was specifically designed for use in dermatology clinics and was based on a large dataset of images of skin lesions that were primarily of Caucasian patients. The findings of the study revealed that the tool performed less accurately on non-Caucasian patients, and that there were a number of instances of misdiagnosis. One of the most concerning findings reported by the authors was that the tool underreported the severity of melanoma in patients with darker skin types, potentially giving users a false sense of security in their diagnosis. Such findings are of particular concern, as they have the potential to lead to decreased rates of early detection, and ultimately to increased rates of morbidity and mortality in affected individuals.

These findings can be corroborated by further reading on the topic. It has been shown that historic underrepresentation of a number of demographics in clinical research data is at the heart of data bias within AI. Thus, not only do a number of fallibilities exist within current health technology, the issues aforementioned within a number of case studies, presented by research groups Liu et al. [16], and Kakish et al. [15], highlight a number of pressing ethical issues regarding health technology within the healthcare system. These are not solely confined to the errors in the health technology and resulting in odious health technology failures; these can and do have the potential to exacerbate and solidify already extant disparities found within the current healthcare system.

Recent studies have begun to highlight the issue of algorithmic bias in skin disease detection, showing that AI systems can perform differently on images of patients with different skin tones. These studies demonstrate that the issue of bias in AI for skin disease detection is real and must be addressed, not just ignored. One study found that a model performed significantly better on images of patients with lighter skin tones than on images of patients with darker skin tones. The study highlighted how the same model can produce different results on different datasets, and how these differences can have real-world consequences for patients. Another study found that AI systems were less accurate at detecting skin lesions on darker skin tones, and that these systems were more likely to misdiagnose certain conditions on darker skin tones. These findings are concerning, as they suggest that AI systems currently in use may not be reliable for patients of all skin tones. They also highlight the need for greater accountability in the development and deployment of AI systems for skin disease detection, including more testing on diverse datasets and greater consideration of how bias may be introduced at different points in the system.

VI. Ethical Dimensions and Responsibilities

A. Developer Accountability

The ethical dimensions surrounding algorithmic bias in AI-driven skin disease detection reflect critical considerations for developers and healthcare providers alike. As AI technologies increasingly inform diagnostic processes, understanding the ethical implications of these systems emerges as paramount for ensuring just and equitable healthcare outcomes. The responsibilities surrounding these technologies extend across multiple domains, necessitating a collaborative approach to ethics in the deployment of AI interventions in dermatology [4].

The ethical responsibility for AI systems for the detection of skin diseases lies with the developers of the AI systems. The ethical accountability of the developers of an AI system for the detection of skin diseases comprises the design, the implementation, as well as the evaluation of the algorithms of the AI system. An ethical accountability of the developers of an AI system for the detection of skin diseases requires an interaction with datasets of different populations for the training of the AI system in order to avoid any population-specific bias and to guarantee equal opportunities for all minority groups [11]. Well-established biomedical datasets of patients of different populations are often not available yet due to the historical neglect of underrepresented populations in biomedical research. Thus, an ethical accountability of the developers of an AI system for the detection of skin diseases requires a thorough scrutiny of the used training data in order to guarantee an inclusive data selection as well as a fair AI system.

The largest ethical risk for the distribution of these dermatological diagnostic tools into the healthcare system will be the risk for these systems to reproduce and/or amplify existing biases and/or discrimination found within current datasets. Several researchers have recently documented the systematic error in the analysis of images of skin of patients of different racial/ethnic groups by currently distributed skin analysis AI systems, as well as other forms of health analysis AI. Such error can and does result in the misdiagnosis of patients, often in a manner that systematically and/or discriminatorily affects minority populations. While these types of biases and/or discrimination are oftentimes the result of the datasets that have been used for the development and training of these systems, they can also be the result of the way in which the systems have been designed. As such, these types of biases and/or discrimination can in some cases be amplified by the use of AI health analysis systems. These types of risk can only be mitigated through the creation of ethical guidelines for the distribution of health analysis AI systems which require these systems to be transparent in their processes and to provide a sufficient level of explainability in order to allow a human healthcare provider to assess the results of any analysis for any potential bias and/or discrimination that the system may have incorporated, as well as for the developer of any health analysis AI system to ensure that the system that they are developing does not and will not distribute health information that contains any bias and/or discrimination.

B. Clinical Responsibility

But perhaps the greatest responsibility to prevent further mistakes in the use of AI in clinical practice is held by individual clinicians. The use of health information technologies in general and specifically of AI health information technologies requires considerable amounts of time and financial resources. There is a danger, therefore, that the reliance on the system's decisions will become routine and natural, to the extent that the clinician will fail to apply their best clinical judgment. There is an added danger that the systems will be unable to identify cases in which application of the system's algorithms would lead to suboptimal decisions. A number of ethical considerations emanate from these concerns, foremost being the need for health IT, including health information technologies that use AI, to be used in ways that are consistent with the ethical principles of beneficence and do not cause harm. The ethical responsibilities of individual clinicians to monitor for and address the potential for AI system failure include exercising clinical judgment to assess information generated by the system, recognizing limitations in the system's capabilities, and reviewing information generated by the system prior to making clinical decisions.

C. Interdisciplinary Collaboration

Interdisciplinary approaches can be applied to developing guidelines for the ethical implementation of AI systems. Those involved in designing AI include ethicists, sociologists, and legal experts as well as data scientists and software developers. Understanding the full impact of bias in the implementation of AI systems in a variety of contexts [4] requires a broad view that can be developed through the input of various stakeholders including dermatologists, engineers, data ethicists, and healthcare managers and a range of other anthropologists studying particular communities of users. Such a collective perspective can facilitate the creation of a set of comprehensive ethical guidelines to be used when developing specific types of AI for use in the field of dermatology.

Another important aspect to take into consideration in the face of the existing challenges related to the mitigation of the biases in the use of AI in healthcare are the multidisciplinary teams that are working in the design of these systems. The collaboration of data scientists, healthcare providers, sociologists, and ethicists is crucial in order to identify the different possible perspectives that need to be considered and the potential biases that could be embedded in the design of an AI system. By taking into account the different possible perspectives and the different possible biases that could be included in an AI system, the collaboration between multidisciplinary teams of experts is key to design an AI system that is able to take into account the experiences of all the patients, and therefore, to design an equitable healthcare system [10].

VII. Regulatory Frameworks and Future Directions

A. Policy and Governance Needs

A final key point of consideration for the use of skin analysis AI in the diagnosis of skin diseases is the regulatory frameworks and ethical guidelines that are currently lacking to govern the healthcare uses of AI. Most notably, policymakers must develop standards for the detection of bias in the clinical use of AI. Such assessment of bias in clinical AI systems will hold the developers of the programs accountable for the performance of their technology. The healthcare system as a whole would greatly benefit by implementing rules for the fair, equitable development of healthcare AI, and by enforcing accountability for programs which perform unfairly for certain populations of patients. Healthcare, by its very nature, involves an inherent inequity in terms of access to and quality of care for different populations. Thus, the implementation of strategies to increase equity in healthcare, such as the use of healthcare IT, must also target disparities and work to create programs which are fair and able to be used by all people, to the best of their ability, in order to improve health disparities in skin disease detection and diagnosis.

Even with current state-of-the-art, the problems of algorithmic bias in AI-driven skin disease detection are not yet fully solved, and with growing self-service healthcare (SSHC), an ever greater focus needs to be placed on in-depth exploration of possibilities for solving problems of algorithmic bias and, in the process, ensuring ethical deployment of intervention in healthcare. The earlier-mentioned improved design, as well as proper and continuous evaluation of dermatology AI [2], [4], also need to be of highest priority in order to also uncover design deficiencies and or new types of bias that had not yet been thought of before and which therefore need to be immediately and adequately addressed as well.

B. Continuous Auditing and Evaluation

A further component to tackle the issues of algorithmic bias in the diagnostics of skin diseases is to implement audits and evaluations of the performance of AI systems in the diagnosis of skin diseases on an ongoing basis [2]. Performance evaluations should be transparent and the involvement of stakeholders from patient communities is essential in the design of AI systems for diagnostics of skin diseases. This will ensure that technology is used to the best of its ability to improve patient care and health outcomes in a variety of patient populations. It is also important for healthcare providers to learn about the potential for bias in AI and how this bias can be managed in order to make the best clinical decisions.

The consequences of algorithmic bias for the detection of skin diseases extend far beyond incorrect detection and potentially neglect of life-threatening diseases. Patients are skeptical of technologies, especially when they receive worse results than other population groups. Even more critical is the patients' perception of their healthcare providers, who presumably are responsible for the application of the AI system. The patients' concern about being misdiagnosed or treated improperly can lead to a variety of negative consequences, such as deterrence from using digital health technologies and consequently from using healthcare services in general. To address these critical concerns, it is essential to study the various social determinants of health and the determinants of unequal distribution of training data for AI models. Addressing these concerns is crucial for the development of health IT applications that improve rather than worsen health inequities.

VIII. Conclusion

In conclusion, to address the problem of the detection of skin diseases by means of AI, a comprehensive approach is necessary. First of all, the datasets which are used for the training of models have to be designed in such a way that they are diverse enough to represent all different skin types and their respective manifestations of diseases. Furthermore, audits and evaluations of health technologies have to be conducted in a fair and inclusive manner in order to identify possible biases and to counteract them.

The lack of skin tones of color in images of skin conditions in databases for the training of AI programs leads to the fact that the algorithms are not able to diagnose skin conditions as accurately in patients with skin tones of color as in patients with light skin. The late and wrong diagnosis of skin conditions can have serious consequences for the health of patients. The consequences of errors in the diagnosis of skin conditions through the use of AI programs can be severe. Therefore, it is necessary to include more images of skin conditions in databases for the training of AI programs, which show patients with different skin tones of color. This is the only way to ensure that the algorithms are able to diagnose all skin conditions as accurately as possible, also in patients with skin tones of color. Only in this way is it possible to achieve equal medical treatment for all patients, also in terms of the use of AI programs. The use of AI programs in dermatology must be designed in such a way that they do not increase existing health disparities, but rather contribute to their elimination.

While more research is required to address the issue of algorithmic bias in the context of AI-assisted skin disease detection, potential avenues for addressing the issue of equity can be explored. Firstly, it will be necessary for future models to be trained on a more diverse and representative dataset, to include a wider variety of individuals of different ethnicities, and with different skin tones and various skin types and characteristics. By increasing the diversity of the training data, future models will have the ability to be more accurately applied to individuals of all different demographics. In order to address these ends, it will be necessary for individuals in a variety of disciplines, including dermatology, computer science, data ethics, and anthropology, to collaborate to develop a number of frameworks that can be used to support the development of AI-assisted dermatological systems that are of high quality and are equitable.

In summary, as the field of AI-powered skin analysis for disease detection is rapidly evolving and has made great progress so far, it is crucial to look at the biases that currently exist in algorithms and to find ways to minimize them in the future in order to provide the best possible healthcare to all humans. This will require commitment to health equity and use of health information technology in ways that actually help to bridge, rather than increase, existing health disparities. With continued study and, most importantly, collaboration among many disciplines, it is possible to develop health information technology that supports the best possible health for all.
