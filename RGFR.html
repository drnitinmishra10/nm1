Research Gaps in Financial Recommendation Systems and the Role of Artificial Intelligence: A Systematic Review

Tamanna Sharma1 and Dr. Nitin Mishra2
1PhD Scholar, Department of Computer Science, Sigma University, Vadodara, Gujarat, India
2Professor, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Parul University, Vadodara, Gujarat, India
Corresponding author: drnitinmishra10@gmail.com

Abstract

Financial recommendation systems (RS) aim to personalize product and service suggestions in domains such as banking, investment, and insurance. Despite the growing use of artificial intelligence (AI) in finance, RS face unique challenges. This review surveys literature on financial RS from 2018–2026 to identify research gaps. We find that financial RS must cope with multi-objective goals, highly dynamic markets, and complex product structures. AI-enhanced approaches (e.g., deep learning, knowledge graphs, generative models) improve personalization and adaptivity, but introduce gaps: limited explainability, potential bias/fairness issues, vulnerability to volatility, data quality concerns, and deployment obstacles. Privacy and regulatory compliance are particularly critical in finance. We categorize identified gaps into a taxonomy of AI/algorithmic, data, and governance dimensions. A comparative table summarizes key studies (methods, domains, noted limitations). Mapping gaps to research questions provides a road map for future work. We conclude with future research directions addressing these gaps, emphasizing interdisciplinary solutions. Our analysis highlights that advancing financial RS requires holistic attention to technical robustness, user trust, and compliance.

Keywords: Financial recommendation · Explainable AI · Fairness · Privacy · Robustness · Recommender systems

1 Introduction

Recommendation systems (RS) filter vast information to match users with relevant items or services. In e-commerce and entertainment, RS are well-established. In finance, RS can guide customers to suitable products (loans, insurance, investments) and help advisors make data-driven suggestions. However, the financial domain has distinct characteristics. Financial products are diverse and complex (e.g., bank deposits, loans, insurance, stocks, derivatives). Users often lack specialized knowledge and need trustworthy recommendations. Financial decision contexts demand high accuracy and trustworthiness, since errors can cause large losses. Moreover, the finance sector is highly regulated: consumer protection, fair lending laws (e.g., ECOA in the US) and data privacy regulations impose strict requirements.

As Wu and Li [1] note, financial RS must handle "multi-objectivity, wide feature space, time sensitivity, and parallel interactive behaviors" — concerns that go beyond typical e-commerce RS. For example, recommending investments involves predicting market trends (time series) while balancing risk preferences (multiple objectives). Thus, financial RS research demands specialized focus.

Recent advances in artificial intelligence (AI) — deep learning, natural language models, graph-based methods — have been applied to finance. These promise to improve personalization by leveraging user transaction histories, social sentiment, news, etc. [3]. Adaptive frameworks use reinforcement learning and streaming data to adjust recommendations in real time [4]. However, AI models (especially opaque ones) raise new challenges. Highly complex models may be accurate but lack interpretability, making it hard to justify recommendations to users or regulators. Bias in training data can lead to unfair treatment of subgroups (e.g., disadvantaged loan applicants). Data quality issues (noisy financial records) can degrade performance. Furthermore, production deployment of AI systems in finance requires robustness to adversarial conditions, drift, and the ability to satisfy audit and compliance demands.

This study conducts a deep review of recent literature on financial recommendation systems, especially focusing on gaps and open issues. We structure the analysis by surveying both academic research and industry perspectives. After a formal literature review, we apply a gap analysis methodology to identify and categorize open problems. We integrate AI-specific concerns such as explainability, fairness, privacy, and data heterogeneity into our framework. Finally, we discuss these gaps in context and propose research questions. This paper is aimed at academic researchers and PhD students interested in AI for financial recommendations, providing a comprehensive map of what is known and unknown, and guiding future exploration.

2 Literature Review

We surveyed recent studies on financial recommendation systems (2018–2026). Key sources include two systematic reviews and several applied papers. Wu and Li [1] provide a comprehensive systematic literature review (SLR) of financial product RS. They analyze 65 papers and note that most focus on three product categories: bank products, securities, and others. Their findings highlight that many models leverage multi-domain features and time-series forecasting to predict price trends. They also note integration of unstructured data (news, text) via NLP to improve accuracy. Importantly, Wu and Li identify that while progress has been made, "each method has its shortcomings" and recommend exploring multi-behavior sequence and multi-task recommendation for finance.

Bonde and Bichanga [2] perform an SLR specifically on using RS in finance and banking. They categorize challenges into three main areas: (i) transparency, ethics, and data privacy; (ii) complex content and multiple user behaviors; and (iii) explainability of AI models. They highlight concrete issues like cold-start problems, personalization gaps, fraud detection, and data privacy barriers. Both reviews signal that fundamental RS challenges (sparsity, cold start) persist, while financial context adds unique dimensions (need for trust, risk modelling, regulatory oversight).

Several applied works illustrate AI usage in finance RS. Chatterjee and Das [3] propose a Generative AI (LLM-based) framework for financial product RS. They combine LLMs, reinforcement learning, and time-series modelling on multimodal user data, embedding differential privacy and fairness constraints. Their experiments (on synthetic datasets) show large gains in relevance (≈30%) and engagement (≈25%) versus traditional methods. Similarly, Shahbazi et al. [4] introduce a Cross-Domain Adaptive Recommender (CDARS) with real-time behavioural tracking and knowledge graphs. They fuse multi-domain signals with sentiment analysis and graph embeddings, along with an explainable learning module. CDARS achieves modest CTR/engagement boosts (~8%) over SOTA baselines. These modern systems aim at hyper-personalization and adaptability, but authors note that explainability modules and ethical AI design are integral parts of their solutions.

On the user and regulatory side, industry reports emphasize explainability. The CFA Institute's Research & Policy Center [7] discusses the "black-box problem" of deep models in finance. They stress that opaque recommendations make it hard to verify fairness or satisfy regulators (e.g., fair lending laws). Their analysis identifies a shortage of evaluation metrics and standards for explanations, and privacy risks if explanations leak data. Fritz-Morgenthal et al. [6] similarly urges a "trustworthy AI" approach: enforcing model validation, fairness/bias testing, and multi-criteria model selection (balancing accuracy vs. transparency/fairness).

Focusing on fairness, Yao and Huang [5] specifically address "financial recommendation engines". They argue that financial RS may perpetuate historical biases, e.g., systematically under-recommending profitable stocks to protected groups. They note legal imperatives: credit laws (ECOA) ban discrimination, so RS must incorporate fairness criteria. While not an SLR, their position paper highlights that fairness in finance RS is both an ethical and legal necessity, pointing to demographic parity, equal opportunity, and counterfactual fairness as research directions.

Overall, the literature shows rapid adoption of complex AI models for financial RS, often improving offline metrics. However, many works acknowledge shortcomings: lack of interpretability, data bias, and neglect of privacy or compliance. 3 Methodology for Gap Analysis

To identify research gaps, we conducted a structured literature survey and gap analysis. We followed a methodology similar to systematic reviews:

  • Scope Definition: We focused on publications (2018–2026) on financial recommendation systems, including academic articles, industry reports, and conference papers. Keywords included "financial recommendation", "recommender finance", "AI explainability finance", etc.
  • Literature Collection: Sources were collected from academic databases (Google Scholar, IEEE Xplore, Springer, etc.), as well as white papers (e.g., CFA Institute, ADB). We prioritized peer-reviewed and primary sources (overviews, reviews, and application papers), emphasizing recent work post-2019.
  • Content Coding: Each source was coded for its focus (e.g., domain of finance, ML technique used, reported limitations) and any stated challenges or future work. Review articles [1], [2] were especially mined for summarized shortcomings.
  • Synthesis: We organized identified challenges into categories (AI/algorithmic, data, user/regulatory, deployment) and formed a taxonomy. We also mapped each gap to potential research questions.

This qualitative gap analysis was guided by methodologies in existing SLRs. Our objective was not merely to list known challenges, but to articulate under-explored issues and propose directions. The resulting taxonomy (see Section 4 and Figure 1) and tables are based on triangulating multiple sources, ensuring that gaps are grounded in cited findings.

4 Identified Research Gaps in Financial Recommendation Systems

The review reveals that financial recommendation systems, especially AI-driven ones, face multi-dimensional gaps. We group them into a taxonomy: (a) AI/Algorithmic gaps, (b) Data and Modeling gaps, (c) User/Governance gaps, and (d) Evaluation & Deployment gaps. Each category encompasses specific issues, as summarized below and illustrated in Figure 1.

4.1 AI/Algorithmic Gaps

Modern recommender models (deep neural nets, LLMs, graph models) yield accuracy but lack interpretability. Financial stakeholders demand explanations: why a loan or investment was recommended. This is the classic "black-box problem" in finance. Existing explainable AI (XAI) methods (e.g., feature-attribution) are limited, and standardized explainability metrics are lacking. Thus, research gap #1 is developing XAI techniques tailored to financial RS (e.g., rule-based proxies, counterfactual explanations) that satisfy regulatory and user needs. Closely related is fairness: algorithms may inadvertently reinforce biases in historical data. Few works have adapted fairness metrics (e.g., demographic parity, equality of opportunity) to recommendation contexts in finance. Ensuring non-discrimination (e.g., compliant with ECOA) is a critical gap. A second algorithmic gap is robustness: financial environments are volatile and adversarial (market shocks, fraudulent actors). Few studies address how RS handle noisy or adversarial inputs. For instance, model drift due to economic changes can degrade recommendations, requiring robust or continual learning methods.

4.2 Data and Modeling Gaps

Financial data come from diverse sources: transactional time-series, user profiles, market news, social media sentiment, etc. Integrating multimodal data remains underexplored. While some models incorporate text (news analysis), there is a gap in fusing modalities (numeric, text, graph) in a cohesive framework. Relatedly, data quality is a challenge. Financial records often contain errors, outliers, and missing entries. Pre-processing and cleansing are crucial but rarely automated; biases in input data (e.g., under-reporting of defaults) can distort learned preferences. As Fritz-Morgenthal et al. [6] suggest, data cleaning should be an integral validation step. Research is needed on denoising and imputing transaction data specifically for RS. Another data gap is the lack of dynamic personalization: user preferences in finance can change rapidly (e.g., risk tolerance after a market crash). Real-time or online learning algorithms that adapt to such shifts (concept drift) are needed. Shahbazi et al.'s [4] CDARS begins to address this with incremental embeddings and behavioral signals, but general solutions for streaming financial RS are scarce.

4.3 User/Governance Gaps

Financial RS must respect privacy and legal constraints. Privacy preservation is paramount, as RS use sensitive data (transactions, credit scores). Traditional centralized models risk data leakage. Federated learning and differential privacy have been proposed for cross-institutional finance [8]. Yet, their use in actual recommender engines is an open area. How to design end-to-end RS that perform well without exposing raw data remains an open gap. Similarly, regulatory compliance and ethics introduce requirements: models must allow audit trails, consumers may require explanations under GDPR/Reg BI, and fairness under financial law. There is little guidance on embedding legal rules into recommendation algorithms or on certifying systems. Another user-related gap is understanding multiple user behaviors, e.g., households or group decisions. Bonde et al. [2] highlight that accounts often have multiple users with interacting preferences. RS must model such group dynamics, which is largely unaddressed.

4.4 Evaluation and Deployment Gaps

Standard RS metrics (precision, recall, RMSE) do not capture financial criteria. New evaluation metrics that combine accuracy with risk-adjusted utility, fairness indices, and explainability scores are needed. For example, a model might increase profit but introduce bias; how to quantify such trade-offs? Research should develop domain-specific benchmarks. Moreover, deployment challenges are under-studied. Integrating RS into real banking/insurer systems requires scalability and monitoring. MLOps practices for finance (model versioning, drift monitoring, incident management) are not well-established. As Fritz-Morgenthal et al. [6] note, tracking model changes and data history is crucial. There is a gap in research on best practices for deploying finance RS in production, including human-in-the-loop interventions and stress-testing under extreme events.

5 Discussion

The identified gaps highlight the complex interplay of factors in financial RS. Many technical limitations noted in general recommender research become even more acute in finance. For example, the well-known cold-start and sparsity issues are exacerbated by the high stakes of wrong predictions. Moreover, financial RS must not only be accurate but also trustworthy. The literature makes clear that building explainability and fairness into models is not optional but central to adoption. Several works (e.g., Chatterjee 2025 [3], Shahbazi 2025 [4]) incorporate such features, but these are initial prototypes. A broader gap is in standardizing and validating these properties across models.

Data-related challenges are also critical. We note a gap in benchmark datasets for financial RS research. Unlike movie or product RS, there are few public financial transaction datasets due to privacy. This hampers reproducibility and comparison of methods. Efforts to create anonymized, realistic datasets (e.g., synthetic customer portfolios or simulated trading logs) would advance the field. Similarly, modeling real human behavior in finance (e.g., risk preferences, goal-oriented investing) is underdeveloped. Most research simulates users or overlooks behavioral economics aspects.

Regulatory considerations cut across all gaps. For instance, even if a powerful deep learning model exists, it may never be used unless it can be audited. Thus the gap in XAI is not theoretical but practical: models need to provide human-comprehensible justifications. Similarly, data privacy laws (GDPR, GLBA) may demand that recommendations do not reveal personal data; this suggests the need for cryptographic or federated solutions to RS. Financial institutions also operate under strict testing regimes; yet little work explicitly targets regulatory-approved validation of RS.

In summary, our survey shows a tension between the promise of AI in finance and the precaution required. Most existing studies focus on improving algorithmic performance; fewer address the broader systems issues. Closing these gaps will likely require interdisciplinary collaboration (AI researchers working with finance regulators, ethicists, and user experts). The taxonomy above can serve as a checklist for researchers designing new financial RS: Have you addressed interpretability? data bias? privacy? metrics? deployment?

6 Future Research Directions

Based on the gaps, we propose several research directions:

Explainable and Fair Models: Develop hybrid RS models that balance accuracy with transparency. This could involve integrating rule-based or neuro-symbolic components, generating counterfactual explanations, and creating fairness regularizers. For example, research could explore using SHAP/LIME in finance-specific settings or designing inherently interpretable models for credit recommendation, as called for by the CFA Institute [7].

Robustness and Adversarial Defense: Study the impact of market volatility and malicious inputs on RS. This includes designing continual learning schemes to adapt to non-stationary financial data, and employing adversarial training to defend against manipulative patterns (e.g., fake reviews or Sybil attacks). Simulation of stress scenarios (like 2008 financial crisis) for RS benchmarking is suggested.

Privacy-Preserving and Federated Learning: Investigate federated or encrypted recommendation models that keep user data local. Building on works like Tam & Corrado [8], one could apply FL to joint recommendation across banks while preserving customer privacy. Evaluating the trade-offs between decentralization and model performance in finance is an open area.

Multimodal and Context-Aware RS: Explore integration of heterogeneous data: news sentiment, social media signals, geolocation (for insurance risks), etc. Techniques like knowledge graph embeddings (as in CDARS [4]) can be extended to financial product ontologies. Multi-task learning frameworks can allow models to handle co-occurring objectives (e.g., recommending products while predicting risk).

Domain-Specific Evaluation Metrics: Design metrics that reflect financial impact. For example, incorporate risk-adjusted return metrics, or fairness metrics that account for regulatory parity (like comparing approval rates across demographics). Develop benchmarks and shared datasets (even if synthetic) for key financial use-cases, enabling meaningful comparisons.

Operational Guidelines and MLOps: Formulate best practices for deploying RS in financial institutions. This includes developing audit processes for RS, guidelines for logging and monitoring, and interfaces for human oversight. Studies on human-AI collaboration (e.g., advisors using RS as decision support) could also be fruitful.

Regulatory and Ethical Frameworks: Collaborate with regulators to define standards for financial RS (e.g., what constitutes an acceptable explanation, privacy thresholds, fairness targets). Creating certification processes (analogous to model risk management) for recommendation algorithms would help bridge research and practice.

7 Conclusion

Financial recommendation systems offer great potential to improve decision-making in banking, investment, and insurance. However, our review finds significant research gaps before this potential is fully realized. Technical innovations in AI must be paired with considerations of interpretability, fairness, and compliance. We have identified key gaps across AI algorithms, data, and governance. Closing these gaps will require novel methods (e.g., XAI, federated learning), new metrics, and cross-disciplinary approaches. By mapping research gaps to specific questions, this paper aims to guide future work. Ultimately, developing trustworthy, adaptive, and responsible financial recommender systems can enhance user trust and financial inclusion. Researchers tackling these challenges should leverage both cutting-edge AI and domain expertise to build the next generation of financial RS.
