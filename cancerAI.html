Abstract
The integration of AI into cancer diagnostics is an evolution from traditional cancer diagnostic methods currently used in clinical practice. There is an urgent need for a number of sophisticated technologies that are able to improve cancer detection and subsequently provide the best possible patient care. Cancer diagnosis traditionally has been conducted by a number of methods, including analysis of imaging studies, evaluation of biopsy results and clinical evaluation. While these methods of cancer detection have proven to be effective, each of the methods have several limitations with regards to the detection and treatment of cancer. There are several challenges to the detection and treatment of cancer including subjective human error, variation in interpretation by different observers and complexity of cancer diagnosis in general. AI has the potential to transform the cancer diagnostics. These tools use large amounts of data and complex algorithms to improve the cancer detection. For instance, the Machine Learning models can process large amounts of the medical images to detect the cancer at the early stages of growth and thus can provide better diagnosis than human doctors. The AI systems have been found to provide better diagnostic results than the conventional cancer diagnostic methods for certain cancers. For example, the AI systems have been found to improve the sensitivity and specificity of breast cancer and lung cancer detection than those of experienced radiologists. However, the implementation of AI for the detection of cancer has several ethical issues, for instance data privacy, informed consent, and bias in algorithms. This paper discusses the transformative role of artificial intelligence in cancer diagnosis, its underlying technologies, clinical applications, ethical considerations, and future directions.
Keywords: Artificial Intelligence, Cancer Diagnosis, Machine Learning, Deep Learning, Convolutional Neural Networks, Natural Language Processing, Ethical Considerations, Early Detection, Precision Oncology

IV. Clinical Applications of AI in Cancer Detection
A. Breast Cancer Detection

For instance, early cancer detection through screening mammograms are analyzed by AI algorithms for the detection of breast cancer. A recent study by Ali et al (2025) discovered that a deep learning-based model could detect breast cancer during screening mammography better than an experienced radiologist. They found that the model could achieve a sensitivity of 95% as compared to an average sensitivity of 86% by human radiologists. This highlights the ability of the computer-assisted detection systems to reduce false-negative results, which can allow for earlier intervention and can alter the trajectory of cancer and its subsequent treatment.

B. Lung Cancer Detection

Additionally, in the analysis of CT scans of the lungs to diagnose lung cancer, AI can significantly cut down the time to provide a diagnosis to patients. Researchers found that in a cohort of 85 patients with CT-detected pulmonary masses, a computer algorithm was able to accurately classify 91 of 85 (77%) as either benign or malignant. In comparison, a group of radiologists were able to accurately classify only 66 of 85 (77%) as either benign or malignant (Hunter et al., 2022). As mentioned before, not knowing whether a mass is malignant or benign can cause a significant amount of stress and anxiety to patients. As a result, using AI to quickly and accurately diagnose the disease can save a patient's life and avoid the need for aggressive treatment.

A notable example of the use of AI in the cancer diagnosis is the work conducted by Mahmood et al. (2021) that utilized a CNN to classify images of lung nodules from CT scans with an accuracy rate of approximately 94%. By significantly reducing the number of false positives that can lead to overdiagnosis and subsequent invasive procedures, such as biopsies, AI-assisted methods can improve the detection of lung cancer and ensure that patients receive the most appropriate treatment in a timely manner. In addition, AI can enhance the ability of a radiologist to detect cancer early resulting in better patient outcomes. This type of technology will continue to play a vital role in the efforts to improve the cancer diagnosis.

C. Gastrointestinal Cancer Detection

Recent technological progress in the field of artificial intelligence has led to an emerging number of new tools for detection and analysis of images from endoscopy investigations. The latest findings obtained with the use of machine learning, particularly confirm the high potential of AI systems for the detection of gastrointestinal cancer. By using a sufficient amount of information from endoscopic images, current models are able to analyze all of the available data far faster than any human and make highly accurate early detection of potentially cancerous growths, including precancerous polyps. The results are then instantly available to enable doctors to take action at the earliest possible moment to implement appropriate treatment in the form of surgery, to remove the polyp in question, for example (Ali et al., 2025).

V. AI in Precision Oncology and Genomic Analysis

AI is also being researched for many other applications, one of them is Precision Oncology by analysis of tumor genomics. Large amount of genomic data has been collected and this large amount of data can be analyzed by AI to make prediction of how patient will respond to certain type of cancer therapy. A study was conducted by Mahmood et al. (2021) to stratify patients with Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer (NSCLC) using AI driven predictive model using genomics information and results showed that the model was able to accurately identify patients who would benefit from Immunotherapy and therefore treatment plan can be designed accordingly to maximize therapeutic benefit for each patient. This type of personalized treatment is how treatment for cancer should be and by the use of AI, Precision Oncology is now a reality and with the help of AI, earlier and more accurate diagnosis can be made and cancer can be treated in more personalized manner.

Moreover, as AI is becoming more sophisticated, it will also become possible to integrate more and more genomic data into the diagnostic models that are developed with the aid of AI. Thus, the potential of AI in terms of the so-called 'personalized medicine' could be further developed by using AI analytics in combination with large-scale genomic sequencing data. The algorithms thus developed will be able to predict not only the risk of cancer for an individual but also the likelihood of a particular patient to benefit from a particular type of treatment as well as the potential side effects of such a treatment. Consequently, a personalized treatment plan could be developed for every individual patient on the basis of his or her individual genetic profile. Studies have already shown that the AI-driven analysis of the genomic sequencing data of cancer patients could lead to a revolution in the field of the so-called 'stratified cancer therapy' and that this could be used to develop new cancer treatments as well as to improve existing ones (Huang et al., 2023).

VI. Ethical Considerations in AI Cancer Diagnostics
A. Data Privacy and Informed Consent

The implementation of AI for the detection of cancer has several ethical issues, for instance data privacy. The way data is collected and analyzed can be against patient rights. Also the issue of informed consent, because the way AI systems are implemented these days, it is hard for patients to understand how their data is being used. Furthermore there is the issue of bias in algorithms. When an AI system is trained on a big data set, there is a big chance that the data set is not diverse enough, which leads to the fact that the AI system makes decisions that are based on already existing health disparities (Dlamini et al., 2020). For instance, an AI system can be trained on images of patients with cancer. These images can be mostly of one ethnic group, which can lead to the fact that the AI system is not able to detect cancer in patients of other ethnic groups. This can cause severe health disparities and can lead to the fact that some patients are not treated as good as other patients (Alshuhri et al., 2024). So it is very important to take these ethical issues into account when using AI for the detection of cancer, because otherwise it can cause more harm than good.

B. Algorithmic Bias and Health Disparities

Another ethical issue with the use of AI in cancer detection is that there is a possibility for bias. There is a fear that the algorithms used by the AI could have been created using historical health care data that contains biases. These biases could have been created by a lack of diversity in the populations that have been studied in the past. For example, a machine learning model may have been trained on a dataset that consisted mainly of data from Caucasian patients, and, as a result, the model could be less accurate when it comes to patients of other ethnic groups (Dlamini et al., 2020). This could lead to a situation in which patients of certain backgrounds are not receiving the same level of care as others. In order to prevent this from happening, the data that is used to train AI models must be diverse, and the algorithms must be tested for any possible bias. This is particularly important in the field of health care, where there are already large health disparities between different demographics. If an AI system were to exacerbate these disparities, it could have very negative consequences (Alshuhri et al., 2024).

C. Accountability and Transparency

Early cancer detection by AI raises critical ethical concerns, which must be addressed soon enough. In diagnostics, more and more decisions are based on algorithms. In these situations, the classic question of accountability is posed again. What happens if an algorithm makes a wrong decision, in particular a wrong one, based on biases that were stored in historical data? This is where all fears of unequal distribution of healthcare and its potentially disastrous consequences for patients come into play. Only the most advanced and best ethics guidelines can guarantee that early cancer detection by AI is in the best interest of all humanity and, more specifically, of all people from all ethnic groups, backgrounds, and geographical locations, i.e., people equally.

However, when using cancer diagnosis with the help of AI it is important to be critically aware of the given ethical concerns. It is known that the algorithms that AI systems use for cancer diagnosis use are based on machine learning and they need large data sets in order to draw precise conclusions. As mentioned before, large data sets of patient data lead to serious concerns with regards to patient privacy and data security. Furthermore, there is a huge risk of given algorithms containing biases, as the used data sets may contain biases that are reflected in the results of the AI system and even reinforce existing health disparities. The AI systems used for cancer diagnosis must therefore be transparent. Patients, doctors, and IT developers must be able to check the systems and discuss them with one another. Only then it can be guaranteed that the used AI solutions are just, understandable and accountable. In the ongoing discussions with regards to the use of AI in health care it is therefore necessary to think about and to implement new rules in order to guarantee the rights of the patients and to promote an ethically responsible use of health care AI.

D. Impact on Healthcare Workforce

Concern for the displacement of some or many health care workers by the increasing use of health care AI is well-founded. There are growing reports that computer-assisted diagnosis (CAD) can actually replace the role of some health care workers in early cancer detection. Indeed, it has been reported that by 2026 many health care roles will become redundant and that professionals will no longer be required in their current form. Furthermore, the reported concern is not confined to the specific scenario of CAD and that the use of health care AI more broadly will also result in the displacement of many health care workers. Although there is concern in relation to the reported impact on the roles of pathologists and radiologists and their potential replacement by the use of health care AI, this is not to say that the same will apply to all health care workers. On the contrary, AI will only enhance the roles of health care workers and in doing so, will actually increase their significance. Thus, while it is essential that health care workers are supported to ensure that the impact on them of the use of health care AI is as positive as possible, their roles will, in reality, become more meaningful and not less so. For this to be the case, however, there is a need for an ethical framework that supports a transition to new ways of working, and that provides opportunities for training and reskilling to ensure that health care workers are able to work effectively with health care AI systems.

VII. Regulatory Challenges and Frameworks

The primary problem with current regulatory frameworks for the evaluation of computer-assisted diagnostic (CAD) tools and other health information technologies, such as AI systems for cancer detection and diagnosis, is the rate of advancement of the underlying technology. Many current regulatory frameworks are designed with a priori to assess whether a given medical device is safe and effective for its intended use. They typically require the submittal of static information regarding the design, testing, and intended use of the product, which are often not well suited to dynamically learning information systems, such as many machine learning models (Pei et al., 2022).

A crucial component to consider is the need for adequate and updated regulation of AI diagnostic algorithms in order to accurately ensure the quality of healthcare. The approval process of AI algorithms must include an assessment of the level of precision and of safety for the clinical use, as well as the possibility of updating and continuous validation in the clinical setting, also in relation to different patient populations.

To continuously monitor the performance of an AI system that has been approved, health regulatory bodies and other organizations must assess the tool on an ongoing basis. However, unlike other class of products, there is currently no framework to assess the performance of an AI tool in the postmarket setting on a continuous basis. Thus, there is a pressing need to develop assessment methods to continuously evaluate AI tool performance after they have been deployed in the market. The development of frameworks and tools to continually monitor, update, and improve the performance of an approved AI system is crucial. Current frameworks are mostly designed for the assessment of a medical device or diagnostic test before it is placed on the market. Ongoing, postmarket assessment of an AI system is required to ensure that the system performs safely and accurately in practical applications (Pulumati et al., 2023).

Furthermore, a major challenge for current regulatory bodies is the establishment of definitions and of categories of AI applications. Many current frameworks fail to differentiate between AI in general and between different applications of AI, such as AI for gaming, for autonomous driving, and for various healthcare applications in healthcare. In order to guarantee effective oversight and to ensure that corresponding regulations can be adjusted to future developments of a specific application, specific categories and corresponding guidelines for the particular field of application need to be developed. These categories and corresponding rules for the oversight of AI applications in cancer diagnosis need to be designed in close collaboration between regulatory bodies, developers of corresponding health IT, as well as clinicians, in order to ensure that the specific challenges in the field of oncology are properly addressed.

VIII. Limitations and Challenges

Also to be discussed in further detail are the potential problems of all new systems, i.e. problems that relate to the limitations and restrictions of the data used for their training as well as of the results that were achieved in the frame of the project. It is generally difficult to guarantee the full reliability of AI-assisted cancer detection as it is not possible to exclude biases or confounding variables entirely. Even if no deliberate attempts to induce bias have been made, there is always the risk that bias is present in datasets used for the training of models. Moreover, there are risks linked to the transparency and interpretability of the diagnostic support and to the potential over-reliance on technological support. These problems must be checked for potential problems on bias, especially for the risk of reproducing disparities in health care, and have to be corrected in order to guarantee equal access to oncological care for all populations (Obermeyer et al., 2019).

There are also challenges in cancer AI which are currently being investigated. The limitations in current technology and also how these can be addressed. Much of the current information about cancer is unknown and therefore the algorithms need to be updated regularly with the new data and also how the new information can be translated into the new treatment modalities. It is through the collaborations between technologists and also health care providers that these tools can be developed to aid in diagnosis in cancer. The primary aim is to make sure that the cancer diagnosis is the best possible.

IX. Future Directions

Looking into the future of AI in cancer diagnosis and treatment a variety of challenges are currently being addressed with new, innovative techniques in the field of deep learning. When integrated with imaging as well as with large amounts of genomic data from cancer patients the potential of cancer diagnosis as well as treatment is expected to dramatically increase. In the next decade, while there are currently various imaging and diagnostic methods in place for a number of different types of cancer, such as deep learning algorithms and methods of next generation sequencing, new, effective methods of cancer diagnosis and subsequent treatment are being currently developed. Such tools are expected to revolutionize in accuracy and in a number of other parameters and enable not only effective, individualized cancer treatment, which will be based on the characteristics of the specific type of cancer as well as on the characteristics of a specific patient, but also provide a variety of new advantages to the oncologists as well as to their patients.

The future for the utilization of cancer detection using AI will look to evolve even further as more advancements in machine learning and big data analysis come into fruition. Furthermore, with current technology it is predicted that AI will be implemented in numerous emerging health technologies such as genomics and personalization of cancer treatment. As genomic data becomes increasingly accessible, the algorithms are able to reveal subtle connections to cancer and thus allow for more precise cancer treatment strategies to be utilized by health care providers.

The future of AI in cancer diagnosis is very promising and is expected to change the face of cancer detection and treatment in the next decade. As technology evolves, there will be more breakthroughs in using AI in cancer diagnosis and treatment. The collaboration between physicians, computer scientists, and ethicists will be crucial in harnessing the potential of AI in cancer diagnosis and in addressing the challenges and ethics of using this technology in cancer diagnosis. The use of AI in cancer diagnosis has the potential of saving lives and improving cancer patients' care and treatment.

X. Conclusion

The evolution of cancer diagnostics using AI has become a cornerstone of future healthcare. This cutting-edge technology will revolutionize cancer detection utilizing the most accurate, efficient, and effective means of diagnosis to date. It is therefore imperative to develop these novel technologies with the greatest of care to ensure that not only are they the most accurate diagnostic tools available but also that they are ethical and have the greatest good for the greatest number. With the numerous applications of AI in cancer diagnostics, the integration of this technology into current cancer diagnostics will result in improved patient outcomes and the development of novel, more effective cancer treatments.

Oncological diagnostics leverage from a multitude of information types, generated by means of different analyses, including imaging, pathological examination of tissue samples and gene analysis. The amounts of information thus generated can be huge, exceeding by far what health care professionals and scientists are able to analyze. In order to improve cancer detection at early stages and support subsequent treatment schedules that are likely to be more effective, sophisticated methods of analysis are clearly required, some of which can already be classified as superior to conventional approaches. An example of the ability of these computer-based systems of analysis to identify very early stages of cancer has been provided by Silva and co-authors (2023), who reported a study, in which an AI-based tool had performed superior to state-of-the-art diagnostic tools for detection of early cancer. As is typical for such pattern recognition systems, they are capable of identifying features or patterns in large datasets, which may have escaped the attention of an observer, thereby opening up new perspectives for the respective health care issues.

Integrating artificial intelligence (AI) into the processes used for cancer diagnosis is revolutionizing cancer care, in terms of cancer detection accuracy as well as early cancer detection. An array of AI applications, most of which are currently under investigation are being investigated for their potential in analyzing a vast array of data obtained from various cancer imaging modalities, as well as from the numerous generated reports from various patients' comprehensive clinical histories (Ghaffar Nia et al., 2023; Kourou et al., 2021). Early and precise cancer detection using AI-enabled cancer diagnostic systems have the capability of surpassing that of human-based cancer detection methods currently in use in a variety of different cancer types (Kourou et al., 2021). The various roles that AI can serve in a variety of cancer diagnostic applications, therefore, could potentially enhance the processes involved in cancer detection, thereby ultimately contributing to cancer care that is far more effective and even more efficient as well as a number of other desirable attributes that are currently not achievable using a number of the current, traditional cancer diagnostic systems. These applications, as previously noted, would be utilized for a number of disparate diagnostic purposes, while utilizing, for example, deep learning-based approaches for processing of very large amounts of data generated from imaging as well as other diagnostic applications (Kourou et al., 2021). The previously mentioned methods for analysis, it should be noted, have been found to enhance cancer detection accuracy, as well as even contribute to cancer detection that is earlier than previously possible (Kourou et al., 2021). These aspects, as previously indicated, all suggest that the processes associated with cancer detection can be enhanced with the use of the previously mentioned AI techniques that are currently under investigation for their potential uses in the diagnosis of various types of cancer, as noted earlier.

It is critical that in order to gain the trust of clinicians and patients, any diagnostic tool powered by AI must be shown to deliver accurate and fair decisions. However, there are significant ethical considerations for the use of AI in health care particularly with respect to use of genomic data and these must be addressed in a responsible manner. To this end, it is imperative to begin to develop a framework to define the development and use of health care AI in order to identify strategies that can be used to foster innovation while ensuring that such technologies are used responsibly and in a manner that protects the interests of patients and clinicians.

There are also many other reasons why healthcare presents particularly difficult ethical problems for AI. Among them are concerns around data privacy, patient rights to information about their diagnosis, and the likelihood of the development of new forms of bias in how different groups are diagnosed and treated by algorithm. Ensuring the collection and use of patient data for training in AI of any sort is entirely within the remit of strict privacy regulations (e.g. HIPAA in the USA), but has of course provided difficult practice issues around data ownership and patient rights in almost every use of data in which information systems have been developed to support work.

The work of Elemento and colleagues and the collaboration with our Clinical Research Unit (E-Rent-G and Sapienza-IRCCS-NRL) was centered upon the development and operational evaluation of a large database of reading mammograms that they were able to process via an AI engine which went on to surpass the sensitivity ratings of a large cohort of human readers. While the majority of AI projects that are currently under development across a wide variety of clinical disciplines, continue to be in development (and thus to be evaluated) primarily within large numbers of clinical cases that are available for the use of investigators, the work reported by the Elemento group constitutes the first report that results derived from a large database of previously read images (the vast majority having been interpreted by large numbers of human readers and accordingly annotated) are not only highly sensitive but also result in a significant decrease in false positives with AI-powered systems able to accurately determine the requirement for biopsy in women presenting for screening having been found to only require such invasive procedures in a fraction of the number of cases in which such procedures would otherwise have been indicated by human readers. As with evaluations reported by Mahmood et al. (2021) relating to the diagnosis of lung cancer AI-powered systems that are capable of the analysis of images have clear applications in a large number of cancer types, and as Elemento and colleagues went on to describe could easily be used by a large number of readers to facilitate accurate assessment and thus early diagnosis of breast cancer, as well as to determine the precise requirements of individual patients with suspected cancer.

To enable cancer diagnosis using imaging, deep learning has progressed the most and, in particular, using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) images can automatically be analyzed by a computer analyzed. Since from a long time on in many countries huge databases with images (MRI, CT-scan, pathologymicrographs) and corresponding reports have been collected by the health care, the big data of these images and reports can be used for training a computer program, in order to improve cancer diagnosis, with high accuracy, even beyond that of a human radiologist. A computer program for the analysis of such images can, for example, automatically detect cancerous regions and at an early stage of cancer alert doctors. The number of false positive and false negative findings can be significantly reduced by using AI for supporting doctors in their diagnostic work. The number of unnecessary biopsies can be decreased as well. Overall, there is a great potential for AI to support doctors, for example in case of screening for cancer. Lung cancer and breast cancer are examples of cancers for which screening by imaging is common place and for which the potential of AI to improve cancer detection has been shown (Kumar et al., 2022).
