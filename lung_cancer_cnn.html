Abstract
Lung cancer remains a leading cause of cancer-related mortality worldwide, with early detection being critical for improving patient survival rates. Computed Tomography (CT) scans have emerged as the primary imaging modality for lung cancer detection, enabling the identification of small nodules and abnormalities. However, radiologist interpretation variability poses significant challenges. This paper presents a comprehensive review of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) in AI-assisted lung cancer diagnosis via CT scans. We examine key architectures including VGG16 and InceptionV3, transfer learning approaches, multimodal frameworks combining CNNs with Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs), and critical challenges such as interpretability, bias, overfitting, and clinical integration. The analysis demonstrates that CNN-based systems achieve detection accuracy comparable to or exceeding experienced radiologists, particularly for early-stage nodule detection. We discuss preprocessing techniques, regulatory considerations, and future research directions including hybrid models and explainable AI. Our findings indicate that while CNNs show tremendous promise for improving lung cancer detection, addressing challenges related to data quality, model transparency, and clinical workflow integration remains essential for successful deployment in healthcare settings.
Keywords: Convolutional Neural Networks, Lung Cancer Detection, CT Scans, Deep Learning, Medical Image Analysis, VGG16, InceptionV3, Transfer Learning, Explainable AI, Multimodal AI

I. Introduction

Lung cancer is a leading cause of cancer deaths worldwide. In 2020, 2.2 million new cases of lung cancer were reported (World Health Organization, 2021). Early detection of lung cancer is key to reducing the mortality rate of the disease, and there is an urgent need for effective early detection methods. The computed tomography (CT) scan has emerged as the primary imaging modality for the detection of lung cancer. CT scans can produce high quality images of the lung tissue, allowing for the detection of small nodules and other abnormalities that may be cancerous (Scrimger et al., 2020). However, the ability to detect cancer from CT images relies heavily on the interpretation of radiologists. While there are many qualified radiologists, there is significant variability in the quality of interpretations between radiologists. The complexity of the images often makes it difficult for radiologists to identify potential problems.

Early and accurate detection of lung cancer is crucial for saving lives and increasing chances of patient survival. Most patients are diagnosed at advanced stages of the disease, which is associated with low survival rates. The five-year survival rate for lung cancer has been reported to vary significantly, with the National Cancer Institute stating that lung cancer patients' survival rates are affected by the disease stage at the time of diagnosis. This underlines the importance of accurate and timely detection and diagnosis of lung cancer. Utilizing the latest technologies, such as deep learning-based algorithms for the analysis of medical images, is increasingly important for improving the quality of lung cancer detection. Specifically, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have been proven to improve the quality of medical image analysis by enhancing the accuracy of radiologists and the speed of image analysis. Their use in the analysis of Computed Tomography (CT) scans for the detection of lung cancer has the potential to improve cancer detection accuracy, thereby increasing the chances of patient survival.

The use of CNNs for the analysis of images in the field of radiology is rapidly becoming the standard for computer-assisted detection (CAD) in a host of clinical applications. As with most computer-assisted technologies, the core function of the human is being removed from the process of analysis, allowing for the processing of vast amounts of data in short periods of time with a high degree of accuracy and reliability. All current studies are indicating that the level of detection and accuracy for the identification of lung cancer by means of CT scans using CNNs is high, equal to and in some cases even superior to that of the human eye and in particular of experienced radiologists, particularly with respect to the detection of small-sized early-stage lung cancer nodules (Liu et al., 2019). Moreover, the use of deep learning models in the analysis of medical images is having a positive impact on increasing the efficiency of assessment by radiologists, thus helping to alleviate the workload of health care workers.

Moreover, screening high risk individuals for lung cancer can also be supported by the use of computerized images to aid early intervention. The need to develop protocols for the use of deep learning systems, to ensure that images and data from patients are used in appropriate ways and that all data privacy concerns are addressed, must not detract from the fact that the most critical factor in determining the effectiveness of CNNs to aid in lung cancer detection is the quality of the annotated training data that is used for the model's training. Currently, there is no general agreement as to how CNNs should be used within the radiological diagnostic process, with some regarding them as 'box intelligent' (i.e., an aid that can be included within the diagnostic decision-making process of the radiologist), whereas others are keen to press for the direct use of such models in the final stage of the decision-making a conclusive diagnosis (i.e., 'intelligent box'). However, both uses of the models are supported in terms of improved lung cancer detection and treatment in terms of improved outcomes for patients with the disease, which is what really matters most.

B. Transfer Learning Approaches

The use of large annotated datasets for training of CNNs for disease detection has given rise to the approach of transfer learning in which a pre-trained CNN is fine-tuned on a new dataset to learn specific features for a given application. The large datasets of images such as those in the Lung Image Database Consortium (LIDC) and the 14 million images in the ImageNet repository can be used for transfer learning. Recently, a number of studies have reported the results of transfer learning of various CNN architectures such as AlexNet, VGG, ResNet, and Inception on CT images from the LIDC database with promising results (Al-Yasriy et al., 2020). This approach has the ability to enable a model to learn from a relatively small number of samples for a given application, thereby decreasing the risk of overfitting of the model to the training data.

III. Comparative Analysis of VGG16 and InceptionV3

There is ample evidence that showcases CNNs, particularly for image classification and ining for images of size less than 256 x 256 pixels. A. VGG16 Architecture

VGG16 is a straightforward architecture which uses 16 layers that are of the following types: convolutional and fully connected. These layers form a very deep architecture where each layer uses uniform and small receptive fields. This architecture is of great value to identify small features of lung structures and in identifying abnormal structures in the images of CT scans. Research work carried out by Sravani et al. (2024) showed that the feature extraction ability of VGG16 helped in detecting of a large number of malignant pulmonary nodule and also showed a sensitive result for the detection of cancerous structure in lung by using the model on external dataset. This model is of less computational power and is not very intensive and hence can be easily run on hardware that are of less configuration and also can be deployed in day to day clinical environment for the screening of lung cancer in large population but a high performance system is required for processing the large amount of medical images for screening of lung cancer in large population.

B. InceptionV3 Architecture

InceptionV3 is a more complex architecture than VGG16 which has varied number of layers. Inception modules are used in this model. The varying kernel sizes used in Inception modules help to capture multi-scale information in the images, such as small texture details, medium-size object features, and large contextual information. This architecture is designed to be computationally efficient. The features extracted from the input images are used to classify images into different classes. The InceptionV3 architecture has been reported to achieve higher accuracy and speed than VGG16 for the detection of lung cancer from CT scans (Klangbunrueang et al., 2025). For instance, the model has been reported to have lower false positive rate which is critical in early detection of cancer. The auxiliary classifiers in InceptionV3 also aid in training the model, thereby helping the model to learn and generalize well for different classes of data and for different patients and their corresponding images. This property of the model is very important for the accurate detection of diseases from images of patients. Therefore, the model can be used for the early detection of lung cancer and for other applications in the field of health care. The use of the model in health care system would aid in the timely treatment of the patient.

IV. Multimodal Frameworks: CNN and ANN Integration

A key advantage of combining of CNNs with ANNs in order to analyze CT scans and other health information in a clinical setting is that, as well as structural information from images, there is a vast amount of other information, for example: patient's history; demographics; results from laboratory tests, etc. in order to form an accurate medical assessment and, most importantly, in order to diagnose whether or not a patient has developed a malignant cancer and of what type. The capabilities of ANNs in learning from data that contains many different features is far greater than those of CNNs, and so, this makes for a powerful and attractive solution, as the networks can learn from images and from other health-related information.

Oncu and Ciftci (2025) provided a recent study relating to the implementation of a multimodal CNN and ANN for the early detection of lung cancer from CT scan images and patient's clinical information, which achieved a higher accuracy for cancer detection by classifying the samples into malignant or benign than the typical methodologies. Their model used a CNN to classify the features extracted from the images of the CT scans while used an ANN to classify the clinical information. By the integration of both models, the performance of cancer detection was found to be improved in terms of sensitivity and specificity in addition to reducing false positive and false negative cases that are critical in the field of cancer.

V. Challenges in Clinical Implementation

A. Overfitting and Data Imbalance

Overfitting of the CNN is another important aspect to consider. Many CNNs have a huge capacity to learn complex models of data, which makes it easy for the CNN to overfit the training data. This results in the CNN performing very well on the training data, but performing poorly on unseen data (Sathyakumar et al., 2020). Overfitting of the CNN is exacerbated by the fact that in many medical datasets, there are a large number of negative cases and a small number of positive cases. This results in the model learning to recognize the negative cases very well and not learning to recognize positive cases as well. This is because there are fewer positive cases for the model to learn from. Regularization, such as dropout, k-fold cross validation, and early stopping, can be used to combat overfitting of the CNN. These methods have been shown to reduce the effect of overfitting and improve the performance of the CNN on unseen data.

B. Interpretability and Explainable AI

However, there is little research into how the decisions made by CNNs can be interpreted. In contrast to other areas of AI research, CNNs are often referred to as 'black boxes' because they function as a 'single, opaque block' that takes images as input and outputs classifications. There is therefore a great need for research that aims to increase interpretability of CNNs and, more generally, of deep learning architectures. The fact that there is much interest in explainable AI (XAI) and that a number of frameworks are being developed to facilitate such research into explainability of AI systems indicates that this is a very active research area. Visualization of the features of the input images that were used by the CNN for making a classification are one of the ways in which the decisions made by CNNs can be made more transparent. It is to be expected that research into the increased interpretability of CNNs will, in the long run, also lead to increased trust of the AI systems that are based on these models by clinicians. Ultimately, it is crucial that clinicians are able to trust the output of AI systems, because they must be able to rely on the output of these systems in their decision-making processes. Moreover, clinicians must also be able to justify the decisions that they have made, also with respect to the output of AI systems. The fact that the output of a CNN is used as input for the decision-making process of a clinician does not mean that the clinician is relieved of responsibility for the decisions that are made. On the contrary, the clinician is, in this case, responsible for ensuring that the output of the CNN is justified. It is therefore of utmost importance that the output of the CNN can be justified. This can be achieved by increasing the degree to which the CNN is interpretable.

C. Bias and Data Quality

As another challenge in using CNNs for lung cancer detection in CT scans of patients, there is a risk for bias. Algorithms that have been trained on large datasets of images to classify features in images can be influenced by existing biases in the data. When for example a large fraction of images of patients with lung cancer have been collected from a relatively young population, then there is a risk of misdiagnosing or underdiagnosing patients that are of older age. This makes it very important to test the algorithm on images of patients of different demographics and also to collect a very diverse set of images of patients. Also, when data is collected from different institutions, it is often very difficult to combine the data due to large differences in how the data was collected. This makes it very difficult to train a single CNN on the combined dataset. There are several ways to deal with this problem, for example by using transfer learning or by sharing the data between researchers in an open science fashion. However, currently, there is a big culture barrier between researchers that use open science and researchers that do not use open science. Changing this culture in order to promote open science is a big challenge by itself.

VI. Image Preprocessing and Enhancement Techniques

Lung cancer, which is a leading cause of cancer deaths worldwide, is often diagnosed at a late stage of the disease, when treatment is less effective. Therefore, the development of an accurate and fast computer-aided system for the detection of lung cancer in CT scans is extremely important. In recent years, deep learning methods, particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), have been widely used for analyzing medical images. Recent advancements in image preprocessing techniques and deep learning models have improved the performance and robustness of CNNs in lung cancer detection.

A number of key enhancements have been developed that are used in order to process the images prior to their use by CNNs in order to enhance their quality. One of the areas that have seen significant amounts of research and development include a number of image processing techniques which are able to enhance the histogram, reduce noise, and enhance the contrast of the CT images. All of these items are able to greatly enhance the ability of CNNs to be able to identify a number of different items of interest within the images including tumors. This is particularly important in early stage lung cancer where there are a number of different subtle changes that occur in order to allow the disease to progress. These subtle changes can be very difficult to identify using standard CT images.

Despite advancements made by CNNs, utilizing these systems in the health care setting raises numerous issues that are to be addressed, primarily by the regulators of health care, for example, FDA. Establishing and enforcing guidelines on health care AI algorithms requires the consideration of various technical, ethical, legal, and social issues. While these systems are able to process large amounts of health care data in a very short amount of time, generating results that can aid health care professionals in diagnosis and treatment of a patient, much of the processes within the system cannot be reverse engineered. VIII. Training and Trust in AI Systems

In addition, training of the medical staff is essential for effective implementation of the AI-assisted diagnostics. The radiologists and the oncologists need to understand the strengths and limitations of the AI algorithms in order to effectively interpret the results of the computer-assisted analysis and to incorporate the results in their own clinical decision making. In order to achieve this, new models of medical education need to be re-designed in order to include an interdisciplinary approach that would combine medical knowledge with the ability to apply the latest technology in the clinical practice. The healthcare professionals would need to continuously update their knowledge and skills in order to keep up with the rapid development of the AI systems and their application in the diagnostics of the lung cancer.

Lastly, there are also concerns with regards to the trustworthiness of AI systems. For the technology to be effective in practice, there is a need for practitioners to rely on it. To that end, there is a need to create a framework for the assessment and feedback of AI systems in real life settings. The most effective way to achieve this is through the co-creation of AI systems with clinical teams. This way, practitioners will feel a sense of ownership towards the technology, which in turn will lead to its increased acceptance in practice.

IX. Future Research Directions

This novel technology will continue to improve in the years to come with challenges that need to be addressed by all stakeholders involved in the development and implementation of this technology. Improved models, increased performance and improved areas of diagnosis will be achieved by the use of convolutional neural networks in the diagnosis of lung cancer and other medical conditions.

Overfitting is another problem CNN models are plagued with as these models are inherently very complex in nature to be able to recognize patterns within a dataset and can 'over fit' the training data provided to achieve very high accuracy scores on a limited set of examples that have been used to train a model. Such over fitted models fail to deliver comparable performance levels on an independent test dataset which consists of entirely new instances, not seen during the model's training, and so can cause problems within the healthcare sector where certain circumstances or situations could occur, with previously seen cases from a dataset not providing sufficient instances or examples that match the new test case being assessed. With the goal of a model achieving high sensitivity to detect cases of cancer and possible other circumstances and scenarios which may give rise to serious health problems within a patient, to deliver a high degree of accurate assessments with all circumstances and situations that have not been encountered before, that have not been used in a model's training to develop the model, within a model. Also of great concern are the factors that are deemed to affect how a deep learning model makes a decision and therefore how a healthcare worker may interpret results, generated from images that have been assessed using AI systems. And so it is crucial for these models to be very transparent, and explainable within a clinical setting as previously stated, so as to confirm all aspects that a healthcare worker would rely on to make the right decision, to achieve improved healthcare and successful patient treatment and care.

Addressing these challenges through interdisciplinary collaboration is key to successfully developing effective models for use in the clinic. Through ongoing partnerships with clinical teams, AI developers can ensure that any models they develop are not only robust for a range of clinical scenarios, but also clinically relevant. Input from healthcare professionals during the feature selection process can aid in developing models that focuses on the most important attributes to aid in a correct diagnosis. By working together to create models that are able to take full advantage of the large amounts of data currently available for use in developing effective lung cancer detection models, there is hope for improved accuracy in the detection and diagnosis of lung cancer, and subsequent patient care.

Future research directions must concentrate on addressing the present limitations and advancing the effectiveness of CNN applications in this field. One significant area of exploration is the development of hybrid models that incorporate a combination of CNNs with other AI techniques, such as reinforcement learning or natural language processing, to improve diagnostic precision and assist in clinical decision support systems. Additionally, conducting longitudinal studies that examine the real-world performance of CNN-based frameworks over extended periods may yield insights into their reliability and effectiveness in diverse patient populations.

X. Conclusion

In summary, performance of convolutional neural networks for the detection of lung cancer in CT scans has been very promising so far. However, several challenges are to be addressed to make them ready for the clinical application. Continuing efforts of researchers are expected to resolve these issues and make the accurate and fast computer-assisted detection a part of clinical practice to improve the cancer detection rate and patient care (Thakral & Gambhir, 2024).

Current CNN architectures like VGG16 and InceptionV3 can be effectively used in lung cancer detection and in improving sensitivity and accuracy in detection of pulmonary nodules using CT scans. The use of these models in clinical setting, however, needs to be addressed such as accessibility, interpretability and fine-tuning of models to specific applications in order to maximize their effectiveness.

However, many obstacles remain for the effective deployment of models in clinical environments. First, the models require large amounts of labeled training data. However, creating sufficient amounts of such data for accurate training of models for lung cancer detection from CT scans is problematic due to several reasons. Furthermore, there is a risk of algorithmic bias when training models for diagnosis of certain diseases. For this reason, the interpretability of models for detection of cancer from CT images, such as InceptionV3, remains an open problem. They are very accurate in their classifications, but they are black boxes, and therefore, it is difficult for a human doctor to trust decisions made by a model. The challenge of AI interpretability in cancer detection is one of the biggest in the field of medical AI today, and it is a challenge that requires to be solved in order for the AI systems to be able to work in collaboration with the human doctors to enhance cancer detection and ultimately to improve the quality of care provided to patients with cancer.
