Artificial Intelligence in Quantitative Finance: A Systematic Literature Review and Thematic Synthesis

Abstract
This systematic literature review examines the rapidly evolving intersection of artificial intelligence (AI) and quantitative finance through analysis of 20 seminal research papers published between 2020 and 2025. The review synthesizes literature across five thematic clusters: (1) Deep Reinforcement Learning for Automated Trading, (2) Large Language Models in Financial Forecasting, (3) Generative AI and Asset Pricing, (4) AI-Driven Risk Management and Compliance, and (5) Human-AI Collaboration in Financial Services. Through comparative analysis of research objectives, theoretical frameworks, methodologies, key variables, and major findings, this review identifies critical gaps including look-ahead bias in LLM forecasting, memorization problems in economic predictions, regulatory challenges in democratized AI, and the need for robust human-AI complementarity frameworks. The synthesis reveals a paradigm shift from traditional model-based finance toward data-driven, AI-augmented decision-making systems, while highlighting persistent challenges in model interpretability, data governance, and ethical deployment. This review provides a comprehensive foundation for future research directions in AI-enabled quantitative finance.
Index Terms—Artificial Intelligence, Quantitative Finance, Deep Reinforcement Learning, Large Language Models, Generative AI, Algorithmic Trading, Financial Forecasting, Risk Management, FinTech

I. Introduction

The integration of artificial intelligence into quantitative finance represents one of the most significant paradigm shifts in financial economics since the development of modern portfolio theory [1]. Over the past five years, the field has witnessed an unprecedented convergence of deep learning, natural language processing, and reinforcement learning techniques with traditional financial modeling approaches. This literature review systematically examines 20 key contributions to understand how AI technologies are reshaping asset pricing, trading strategies, risk management, and financial decision-making.

The reviewed literature spans from foundational works in 2020, such as the FinRL library for deep reinforcement learning in automated trading [2], to cutting-edge 2025 research on DeepSeek's democratization of AI in FinTech [3]. The temporal distribution reveals an accelerating research trajectory, with 70% of the reviewed papers published between 2023 and 2025, reflecting the rapid evolution of the field following breakthroughs in large language models (LLMs) and generative AI.

This review addresses the following research questions: (1) What theoretical frameworks underpin AI applications in quantitative finance? (2) What methodologies are employed across different AI-finance research domains? (3) What are the major findings and their implications for market efficiency and financial decision-making? (4) What critical gaps remain in the literature, and what future research directions emerge?

II. Methodology

This systematic review employed a comprehensive search strategy across major academic databases including SSRN, arXiv, NBER, IEEE Xplore, and peer-reviewed journals. Selection criteria focused on papers that: (1) explicitly address AI/ML applications in finance, (2) provide empirical or theoretical contributions, (3) span diverse sub-domains including trading, asset pricing, forecasting, risk management, and regulatory compliance, and (4) represent high-impact contributions as evidenced by citation metrics and publication venues.

Each paper was analyzed using a standardized extraction framework covering: complete citation, research objective, theoretical framework, methodology, key variables, major findings, identified gaps, and author conclusions. Thematic clustering was performed using content analysis to identify recurring patterns, theoretical convergences, and methodological innovations [4].

IV. Thematic Synthesis and Cluster Analysis

A. Thematic Cluster 1: Deep Reinforcement Learning for Automated Trading

Core Papers: Liu et al. (2020) [2]; [Clustering-based Pairs Trading] (2023) [7]; Cont & Vuletić (2024) [8]

This cluster is anchored in Markov Decision Process (MDP) theory, extending traditional stochastic control frameworks to high-dimensional, non-linear trading environments [2]. The theoretical evolution moves from model-based (Black-Scholes, mean-reversion) to model-free reinforcement learning approaches that learn optimal policies directly from market data.

Methodological Progression: FinRL [2] establishes the foundational infrastructure with three-layer architecture (environment-agent-application), implementing seven state-of-the-art DRL algorithms (DQN, DDPG, PPO, SAC, A2C, TD3) with market friction incorporation. The clustering-based pairs trading approach [7] extends DRL to statistical arbitrage through unsupervised learning, introducing partial correlation-based distance metrics that achieve annualized Sharpe ratios of 0.72-1.0. Data-driven hedging [8] advances to generative model-based hedging using VoLGAN for implied volatility surfaces, enabling nonparametric risk minimization that outperforms traditional delta-vega approaches.

Inter-Study Relationships: These studies support each other in demonstrating that DRL can successfully handle financial decision-making under uncertainty, while extending applications from single-stock trading to pairs strategies and derivative hedging. However, they collectively identify the gap between backtesting and live trading performance, with no study providing out-of-sample live market validation.

Critical Gap: The absence of reinforcement learning applications that simultaneously handle multiple asset classes across different time horizons while incorporating macroeconomic regime changes.

B. Thematic Cluster 2: Large Language Models in Financial Forecasting

Core Papers: Lopez-Lira & Tang (2023) [10]; Lopez-Lira, Tang & Zhu (2024) [11]; [Look-Ahead Bias] (2024) [13]; Yang, Liu & Wang (2023) [12]

This cluster operates at the intersection of information economics, natural language processing, and market efficiency. The central theoretical tension is between the efficient processing of textual information and the risks of data contamination and look-ahead bias [11], [13].

Methodological Progression: Lopez-Lira & Tang (2023) [10] demonstrate that GPT-4 achieves approximately 90% hit rates in predicting market reactions to news, with stronger predictability for small stocks and negative news, establishing LLMs as emerging financial reasoning engines. However, Lopez-Lira, Tang & Zhu (2024) [11] fundamentally challenge these findings, proving that LLMs perfectly recall exact economic values before knowledge cutoffs, making backtesting results non-identifiable between skill and memorization. FinGPT [12] responds by democratizing financial LLMs through open-source frameworks, achieving 0.882 F1 scores on sentiment analysis at 1/100,000th the cost of proprietary models.

Inter-Study Relationships: These studies present a dialectical progression—initial optimism about LLM forecasting capability [10] is challenged by methodological critiques [11], [13], which in turn drives innovation in open-source alternatives [12]. The Memorization Problem [11] directly contradicts the validity of historical backtesting in ChatGPT Forecasting [10], while FinGPT [12] attempts to circumvent proprietary limitations through transparent, customizable architectures.

Critical Gap: The field lacks robust methodologies to distinguish genuine LLM reasoning from memorization in financial forecasting, and no standardized framework exists for evaluating LLM performance in true out-of-sample, post-knowledge-cutoff environments.

C. Thematic Cluster 3: Generative AI and Asset Pricing

Core Papers: Kelly et al. (2023) [5]; Kelly, Malamud & Zhou (2024) [9]; [Generative AI in Financial Economics] (2024) [15]; Novy-Marx & Velikov (2024) [16]

This cluster examines the "virtue of complexity" in financial modeling—whether increasingly sophisticated AI models improve or degrade asset pricing accuracy. The theoretical foundation spans statistical learning theory, the bias-variance trade-off, and the Stochastic Discount Factor (SDF) framework [5], [9].

Methodological Progression: Kelly et al. (2023) [5] introduce transformer-based AIPM within the SDF framework, demonstrating 2.4-4.7 percentage point improvements over traditional factor models through cross-asset attention mechanisms. Kelly, Malamud & Zhou (2024) [9] temper this optimism, showing that in noisy financial environments, excessive complexity harms rather than helps prediction accuracy. Novy-Marx & Velikov (2024) [16] demonstrate the industrialization risk: LLMs can generate hundreds of plausible academic papers, enabling HARKing (Hypothesizing After Results are Known) at scale.

Inter-Study Relationships: The AI Asset Pricing [5] and Limited Virtue of Complexity [9] papers present a direct theoretical contradiction—one advocates for complexity through transformers, while the other warns against it in noisy environments. This tension is resolved by recognizing that complexity's virtue depends on signal-to-noise ratios and market regimes. The AI-Powered Scholarship paper [16] extends this by showing that generative AI can both enhance and corrupt the research process itself.

Critical Gap: No unified framework exists for determining optimal model complexity conditional on market noise regimes, and the research community lacks defenses against AI-generated research pollution [16].

D. Thematic Cluster 4: AI-Driven Risk Management and Compliance

Core Papers: Cont & Vuletić (2024) [8]; [Fintech AML] (2024) [19]; Wüthrich et al. (2023) [20]; Patil & Mailcontractor (2024) [18]

This cluster applies AI to traditional risk management functions—hedging, anti-money laundering, actuarial reserving, and operational risk—shifting from rule-based to learning-based approaches [18], [19].

Methodological Progression: Data-driven hedging [8] uses generative models for derivative risk management. The fintech AML approach [19] employs federated learning and graph neural networks for financial crime detection, reducing false positives while preserving privacy. Wüthrich et al. (2023) [20] provide comprehensive educational frameworks for integrating ML into traditional actuarial science. Patil & Mailcontractor (2024) [18] survey transformation across banking, investment, and insurance domains.

Inter-Study Relationships: These studies collectively demonstrate AI's applicability across the risk management spectrum, from market risk (hedging) to operational risk (AML) to insurance risk (actuarial). They support the conclusion that AI enhances risk detection capabilities while introducing new model risks [18].

Critical Gap: Regulatory frameworks lag behind AI capabilities, creating compliance uncertainty; explainable AI requirements conflict with complex model architectures; and cross-border data sharing for AML remains legally constrained [19].

E. Thematic Cluster 5: Human-AI Collaboration in Financial Services

Core Papers: Choi & Xie (2024) [6]; Melnychenko (2020) [22]; Krause (2025) [3]; [Natural Language of Finance] (2023) [14]

This cluster explores the complementarity between human expertise and AI capabilities, challenging the replacement narrative with an augmentation perspective. The theoretical foundation draws from human-computer interaction, bounded rationality, and organizational behavior [6], [22].

Methodological Progression: Choi & Xie (2024) [6] provide the most rigorous field evidence: 277 accountants across 79 SMEs show 55% productivity gains with 8.5% task reallocation from routine to high-value work, with experienced professionals selectively intervening based on AI confidence scores. Melnychenko (2020) [22] argues that AI cannot replace human intuition in holistic financial security assessment, advocating hybrid approaches. Krause (2025) [3] examines how democratized AI lowers entry barriers while requiring human oversight for security and governance.

Inter-Study Relationships: These studies consistently support the "augmentation over replacement" thesis. Choi & Xie's field evidence [6] empirically validates Melnychenko's theoretical argument [22] about human-AI complementarity. Krause [3] extends this to the systemic level, showing that democratization requires human governance frameworks.

Critical Gap: Lack of standardized metrics for measuring human-AI complementarity; insufficient research on optimal task allocation algorithms; and limited understanding of how expertise levels moderate AI effectiveness [6].

V. Cross-Cutting Synthesis

A. Areas of Agreement

1) AI Democratization: Multiple papers [2], [12], [3] converge on the imperative to democratize AI tools for financial applications, reducing barriers to entry for researchers and practitioners.

2) Data Quality Imperative: Across all clusters, the importance of high-quality, curated data is universally acknowledged. FinGPT's data-centric approach [12], FinRL's market friction incorporation [2], and the Memorization Problem's critique [11] all center on data integrity.

3) Market Friction Reality: Studies incorporating transaction costs, liquidity constraints, and bid-ask spreads [2], [8], [7] consistently find that realistic market frictions significantly impact strategy performance.

4) Human Oversight Necessity: Despite AI capabilities, all studies acknowledge the continued need for human judgment, whether in selective intervention [6], holistic assessment [22], or governance frameworks [3].

B. Areas of Contradiction

1) Complexity vs. Simplicity: Kelly et al. (2023) [5] advocate for transformer-based complexity in asset pricing, while Kelly, Malamud & Zhou (2024) [9] demonstrate the "limited virtue of complexity" in noisy environments. This contradiction suggests regime-dependent optimal complexity.

2) LLM Capability vs. Reliability: Lopez-Lira & Tang (2023) [10] demonstrate strong LLM forecasting capability, while Lopez-Lira, Tang & Zhu (2024) [11] prove these results may reflect memorization rather than skill. This fundamental tension remains unresolved.

3) Automation vs. Augmentation: While some studies focus on full automation [2], [5], the Human-AI cluster [6], [22] emphasizes augmentation, creating a spectrum rather than binary choice.

C. Extensions and Evolution

The literature demonstrates clear evolutionary patterns: 2020-2021 focused on infrastructure development [2]; 2022-2023 on capability demonstration [10], [12], [5]; and 2024-2025 on critical evaluation and methodological refinement [11], [9], [6].

B. Proposed Future Research Directions

Direction 1: Causal Identification Frameworks for LLM Financial Forecasting. Develop methodologies that can definitively distinguish between LLM memorization and genuine economic reasoning. Potential approaches include controlled information environment experiments, adversarial testing with synthetic financial data, counterfactual forecasting with manipulated temporal boundaries, and embedding space analysis for memorization detection [11], [13].

Direction 2: Regime-Conditional Model Complexity Optimization. Create adaptive frameworks that dynamically adjust model complexity based on real-time signal-to-noise ratio estimation, including market regime detection algorithms, complexity penalty functions conditional on volatility, online learning with complexity regularization, and ensemble methods combining simple and complex models [5], [9].

Direction 3: Live Market Validation Infrastructure. Establish collaborative platforms for real-world validation of AI trading strategies, including paper trading integration with live market feeds, regulatory sandboxes for DRL strategy testing, standardized performance benchmarking protocols, and risk management frameworks for autonomous trading systems [2], [7].

Direction 4: Cross-Domain Human-AI Complementarity Metrics. Develop quantitative measures of effective human-AI collaboration, including task-specific complementarity indices, expertise-dependent AI utility functions, real-time intervention optimization, and cognitive load measurement during AI-assisted decision-making [6], [22].

Direction 5: Ethical AI Governance for Financial Research. Create frameworks to prevent AI-generated research pollution, including authentication protocols for human vs. AI-generated research, replication standards for AI-assisted findings, peer review adaptations for AI-generated content, and journal policies for AI disclosure and validation [16].

Direction 6: Emerging Market and Inclusive Finance Applications. Extend AI-finance research beyond developed markets through low-resource language financial NLP, mobile-first AI financial services, microfinance optimization with limited data, and cross-border regulatory harmonization for AI deployment [17], [3].

VII. Conclusion

This systematic review reveals that artificial intelligence is fundamentally reshaping quantitative finance across multiple dimensions—from automated trading and asset pricing to risk management and human-AI collaboration. The literature demonstrates both remarkable capabilities and significant limitations of AI in financial applications.

The five thematic clusters identified—Deep Reinforcement Learning for Automated Trading, Large Language Models in Financial Forecasting, Generative AI and Asset Pricing, AI-Driven Risk Management and Compliance, and Human-AI Collaboration—represent distinct but interconnected research trajectories. While early literature (2020-2022) focused on capability demonstration and infrastructure development, recent work (2023-2025) has shifted toward critical evaluation, methodological refinement, and governance considerations.

Several critical insights emerge from this synthesis: First, the tension between model complexity and predictive accuracy is context-dependent. The "virtue of complexity" is real but limited, requiring regime-conditional optimization rather than universal adoption of the most sophisticated models [5], [9]. Second, the memorization problem poses an existential challenge to LLM-based financial forecasting. Until methodologies can definitively distinguish memorization from reasoning, historical backtesting of LLM predictions remains fundamentally compromised [11]. Third, human-AI complementarity is not merely a theoretical ideal but an empirically validated reality. The 55% productivity gains and 8.5% task reallocation documented in accounting settings demonstrate that AI's greatest value lies in augmenting rather than replacing human expertise [6]. Fourth, democratization of AI in finance—exemplified by FinRL [2], FinGPT [12], and DeepSeek [3]—creates both opportunities for innovation and risks of systemic vulnerability. Lower barriers to entry must be balanced with enhanced governance frameworks. Fifth, the research integrity implications of AI-powered scholarship represent an emerging crisis requiring immediate attention. The industrialization of HARKing threatens the credibility of financial economics as a discipline [16].

Future research must address these gaps through interdisciplinary collaboration spanning computer science, financial economics, law, and ethics. The field stands at an inflection point where technological capabilities have outpaced methodological rigor and regulatory frameworks. Closing these gaps is essential for realizing the transformative potential of AI in quantitative finance while maintaining market integrity, research credibility, and human welfare.

The reviewed literature collectively suggests that the future of quantitative finance lies not in choosing between human judgment and artificial intelligence, but in designing sophisticated complementarity systems that leverage the strengths of both [6], [22]. Achieving this synthesis represents the defining challenge for the next generation of financial economists and AI researchers.
