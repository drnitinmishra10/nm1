Abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) is increasingly being applied in skin disease research, offering transformative potential for diagnostic accuracy, individualized treatments, and predictive analytics in dermatology. The analysis of large-scale clinical data from images, electronic health records, and genomic sources through machine learning and deep learning methods enables the development of innovative diagnostic tools that can improve health care delivery worldwide. However, significant challenges must be addressed to realize this potential fully. This paper examines the core challenges facing AI implementation in dermatology, including data quality deficiencies, algorithmic bias affecting underrepresented populations, interdisciplinary collaboration barriers, and the need for robust external validation. We review strategies for mitigating these challenges, including data augmentation, transfer learning, multi-modal frameworks, fairness constraints, and synthetic data generation. The findings underscore that interdisciplinary collaboration among dermatologists, computer scientists, and data analysts is essential for developing equitable, robust, and clinically applicable AI systems. Addressing data quality and algorithmic bias through diverse datasets, standardized protocols, and continuous monitoring will be critical for ensuring that AI-driven dermatological solutions benefit all patient populations equally.

Index Terms—Artificial intelligence, dermatology, skin disease diagnosis, algorithmic bias, data quality, deep learning, interdisciplinary collaboration, health equity, external validation, transfer learning

I. Introduction

AI for Skin Disease Research: Why Are There Challenges That Must Be Addressed to Improve Health Care in Dermatology? Skin disease research involves an increasing number of applications of artificial intelligence (AI). The large amounts of clinical data from images, the electronic health records and from the genomes of patients with skin diseases can be analyzed using machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) methods to create innovative diagnostic tools, individualized treatments and predictors that can improve health care in dermatology and potentially reach all people worldwide (Jaiyeoba O et al., 2024) [1]. In addition to possibly faster diagnoses and fewer false diagnoses than by clinicians, the potential of the vast number of data analyzed by AI systems to discover connections and patterns that humans have not yet detected will greatly benefit health care in dermatology. However, also new challenges for dermatology must be addressed by AI applications for skin disease research.

II. Core Challenges in AI for Skin Disease Research

A. Data Quality and Representation

One of the core challenges for the use of AI in skin disease research is the quality of the used data. This quality refers to the amount, correctness, and coverage of the used data as well as the possible presence of bias. The performance of a model is often directly tied to the quality of the used data and consequently to the quality of the content of the training data set. Hence, a wide variety of different situations such as diverse skin types or colors as well as different types of skin lesions and several other conditions need to be covered in the training data set to ensure sufficient generalizability of the AI model for classification and diagnostics of skin diseases in various situations of application. Most of the available datasets in dermatological research, however, are biased (Melarkode N et al., 2023) [2] and show significant underrepresentation of several ethnic groups. This kind of data sets might result in inferior diagnostic accuracy of models for skin disease diagnostics and thereby in less beneficial treatment for patients from underrepresented demographics. This situation not only results in suboptimal healthcare for underserved populations but also poses serious challenges for the ethical usage of AI in healthcare, because it has the potential to further worsen health disparities for different demographics when not addressed.

B. Algorithmic Bias and Health Disparities

Algorithmic bias occurs when there is a difference in performance of a particular algorithm for different groups of people and can stem from a variety of sources, including data selection bias, feature selection, and developer bias. The risk of biased algorithms in healthcare is that they can lead to the identification of disproportionate risk factors for certain groups of people, or even lead to over- or under-diagnosis of diseases in different demographic groups. A number of challenges must therefore be addressed in order to ensure that AI systems can be safely implemented in the clinic and that they do not lead to any further health inequity. First, there is a need for the rigorous validation of AI algorithms, including assessment of performance in diverse populations, as well as on-going monitoring of their performance in real-world implementation. There is also a need for developers of health AI to be aware of the potential for bias, and to take steps to minimize it.

Challenges in AI adoption for the analysis of skin diseases will need to be addressed by inter- and transdisciplinary teams of individuals with in-depth expertise in skin disease, including clinical dermatologists as well as in the use of data, particularly from healthcare. Additionally, experts in computer science, the analysis of large amounts of data and in methods of statistical analysis and mining of large datasets will be needed to assess the results from the application of models for AI to a given problem in dermatology. Existing problems that are to be addressed, include a lack of understanding and ignorance of potential barriers to effective inter- and transdisciplinary collaboration, between clinicians and data-analysts or computer-scientists, e.g. as a result of a lack of common ground or of misunderstanding due to different terminology, a knowledge gap or even a professional or disciplinary 'siloes'. Interdisciplinary collaboration within and between institutions around the world is needed to develop skin disease models of highest quality, with the strongest possible evidence, in order to support effective AI in a dermatological clinical setting, with high quality data and with methods and measures for assessing results of the greatest possible validity. If all these aspects are effectively addressed, there will be great potential for the development of AI-based computational models that will analyze data to assist in the precise diagnosis and treatment of skin diseases in a timely manner to achieve best possible patient outcomes.

III. Dataset Limitations and Demographic Representation

Another important factor that contributes to the problem of poor performance of models trained on existing datasets is that the images used in the dataset can fail to recognize or accurately classify a condition that is less represented in the images used in the development of the model. In a recently published article, the authors Escalé-Besa and coauthors used a diverse dataset of images used for the training and testing of a deep learning model. The authors acquired images sourced from academic medical centers around the world and found that the images fail to reflect the types of cases seen in diverse populations in outpatient dermatology clinics. They found that common presenting lesions were under-represented in the images. In conclusion, images in many currently available datasets fail to be adequate to train models to accurately classify lesions for diverse populations of patients.

The challenges that face health care of high quality of data and large databases with different views, also created new strategies for improving the data and the results that are achieved with the use of information systems, with the aim of face new problems and to increase the quality of learning of the models in order to be able to improve the results in the diagnosis of diseases of the skin, thus, there are strategies to face the problem of the low quality of data, such as: data augmentation, with the aim of to improve the results of learning of models by generating new images that are obtained from existing images of the database, in order to allow the models to learn new characteristics that are present in images of different variants, such as: changes of scale, rotation, changes of colors, etc. Thus, Fliorent et al. (2024) [3] said that: "there are several techniques to improve results by using information systems, including the use of data augmentation, that enables to increase the number of images in a database by generating new images that are different from the images that already are registered in the database, in order to improve the learning of models that are used in order to identify and diagnose diseases of the skin, and to achieve better results in the diagnosis of diseases of the skin by means of the information systems, also it is recommended to use datasets that contain different varieties of demographic groups, and of types of skin, and presentations of diseases of the skin, in order to improve the results of the AI systems, as said Escalé-Besa et al. (2024) [4].

V. Impact of Algorithmic Bias on Health Equity

The implications of AI biases can be severe. Like in any sector of human life, also in healthcare, there are existing inequalities of access to care, and it seems that in case of dermatological AI solutions, they might be even exacerbated. As reported by Liopyris et al. (2022) [9], the currently available tools, which are mostly white, might underestimate the prevalence and severity of diseases in darker skin tones, missing even life-threatening conditions and thus potentially worsening current discrepancies in healthcare access for underrepresented groups. These missteps could lead to life-altering errors in care—delayed or missed diagnoses, or worst, complete dismissal of a helpful tool by populations for which it was intended to aid in their care. The bias in current dermatologic AI could compound inequities in healthcare and lead to suboptimal care for patients who already struggle to access needed resources.

The impact of algorithmic bias can extend even beyond possible misdiagnosis, and affect recommendations as to the best treatment. By potentially leading to poor outcomes for individuals from different backgrounds, such AI in dermatology could exacerbate rather than solve current healthcare inequities. Therefore, it is not merely the responsibility of the healthcare provider to implement a safe and effective diagnosis and treatment plan, but also the developers of the AI algorithms used in these decisions. Bias must be removed in order to create the potential for truly equitable healthcare for all.

VI. Redresses to Algorithmic Bias in Dermatology

As the field of dermatology works to integrate advanced computational systems for assisting with diagnosis and patient care, many current models are inherently affected by a host of biases from various levels in the system. Redressing these sources of inaccuracy is challenging but can be addressed if a concerted, multifaceted effort is undertaken that prioritizes data diversity when training models of artificial intelligence for health care. In order to develop an optimal model of AI, to be used for a myriad of diverse dermatological conditions and across a broad and diverse demographic, creating data sets that mirror the innumerable variables found in human skin and effectively using this information within a variety of models with end-user involvement for the fine-tuning of these computational systems will be crucial in increasing accuracy and avoiding a number of additional biases that could be hidden within the algorithm. This will involve various disciplines including medicine, information technology, and data analysts in an attempt to tackle the challenges posed by currently available data, which contains an incomplete or inaccurate representation of many patients and consequently a suboptimal amount of information upon which a computer can make an accurate and fair decision.

VII. External Validation and Collaborative Research

A. Importance of External Validation

Conclusion – Addressing The Bias In Health Care Through Collaboration, The external validation of artificial intelligence (AI) models presents a crucial avenue for addressing prevalent challenges in data quality and algorithmic bias within skin disease research. Systematic reviews and empirical studies focusing on the effectiveness of AI systems across diverse datasets and populations have emerged as pivotal methodologies in evaluating the robustness and generalizability of these models.

Collaboration among the various groups involved in developing a number of deep learning architectures for melanoma detection include involvement of a number of dermatologists. These individuals bring to the table clinical knowledge pertaining to a variety of features observed in images of skin lesions. They have the ability to judge whether a feature of interest has been included in the architecture as well as to aid in determining whether the model has been trained and tested on representative data. The work of Grzybowski and colleagues in developing deep learning models for detection of melanoma using images of skin lesions, and in formulating a number of datasets for training of their models, is an excellent example of this collaboration. Involvement of a number of dermatologists in this work was essential in formulating a number of high quality datasets that included a large number of images that had been annotated by the dermatologists. The resulting models, which had been trained and tested on these datasets, had been shown to provide a high degree of sensitivity and specificity for detection of melanoma using images of skin lesions.

B. Role of Computer Scientists and Data Analysts

The contributions of computer scientists are many-fold; they can design efficient algorithms, implement them as robust software, and work with dermatologists to ensure the algorithms are implemented in a manner that is best suited to the clinical requirements. Anand and Dhariwal (2021) [6] described the use of a deep learning-based convolutional neural network (CNN) for the detection of psoriasis from images. The authors described a synergy between computer scientists and a dermatologist, where the model was iteratively refined to improve accuracy based on clinical feedback. Thus, the model was able to improve its performance in a real-world setting.

The fourth key actor involved in dermatology research with AI are data analysts. They work with large datasets, for example, to determine the accuracy of diagnostic results or to identify sources of error or bias. They have a huge added value when combined with clinical experts and computer scientists, because together they are able to provide a sound statistical interpretation of results generated by AI systems and to apply this in order to gain new insights and to improve care and in order to analyze the large amounts of data generated by AI systems and to provide clinical relevant results and in order to study the large amounts of data that contain information about the incidence and prevalence of skin diseases, and that are needed for early detection and for better treatment of skin diseases.

C. Fairness Constraints and Inclusive Model Development

The challenge of algorithmic bias, on the other hand, can affect the performance of any model on a given task. However, While developing models to tackle skin diseases and their corresponding conditions, researchers often depend on a homogenous dataset comprised of images that generally lack diversity and fail to sufficiently represent patients across a broad spectrum of demographics. When these models are deployed on images, the lack of representation from different data sets could result in poor performance. In such instances, it becomes necessary to integrate fairness in algorithmic evaluations by employing techniques that include fairness constraints. Using such constraints can enable researchers and developers to specifically identify and counteract any inherent bias found in models when evaluating performance, thereby ensuring equal performance on relevant data sets for all different categories of individuals. As developers of dermatologic AI models, it is imperative for researchers to emphasize inclusivity and harness the unique attributes of diverse data sets in skin disease research when validating models and developing corresponding data sets for future use.

The collaborative efforts of dermatologists, computer scientists, and data analysts are pivotal in creating models that are not only scientifically robust but also clinically applicable. VIII. Systematic Reviews and External Validation Studies

In recent years, a growing body of evidence has been compiled by researchers including Fernandes et al. (2025) [10] in a systematic review focusing on the external validation of various existing and cutting-edge AI applications in dermatology. A number of trends and pitfalls in current AI models have been revealed by the systematic review, including the most common current approach of training on a homogenous dataset of images of patients of primarily fair skin tones. These systems fail to perform as expected on images of patients with darker skin tones, highlighting a major limitation to using such models in health care settings in order to attempt to improve diagnostic accuracy. The findings of the review suggest that in order to perform external validation on current AI models, a number of diverse datasets will need to be compiled and analyzed in order to assess the strengths and limitations of various models in order to assist in clinical decision making and to provide a more thorough and complete assessment of the performance of various AI applications currently under development for use in dermatology.

In a related study, Yotsu et al. 2025 [19] highlighted the importance of using external validation to counteract AI algorithm bias. They underscored the fact that data for the training a dermatological AI model should be collected from multiple institutions and from different settings. Using an external validation approach, the study fine-tuned a melanoma detection AI model on a large amount of real-world data, collected from various environments, thereby allowing the model to be used for detection of melanoma in different settings. The study emphasized the need for external validation of a large number of high quality datasets, and the need for standardization of data collection methodologies to ensure that differences in results are not due to differences in data collection.

IX. Innovative Approaches and Emerging Solutions

A. Synthetic Data Generation and Augmented Datasets

New research addresses the problems in the skin disease research field by proposing various approaches for dealing with the usage of information processing technologies. While there are the problems of data quality, bias, and collaboration with interdisciplinary fields as well as the problems in health care, the various approaches with the innovative usages of the information processing technologies have been proposed. Those approaches not only improve the accurate diagnoses for the patients with various skin diseases, but also enable the detailed observation and the long term management for the patients.

Augmented datasets have been proposed to fight against challenges of skin image classification using AI. Such platforms can generate high volume of realistic images that would be extremely difficult to obtain otherwise. These images could be used to increase the size of the training datasets for different skin conditions and thereby prevent overfitting of the models. The use of synthetic data has the added advantage of minimizing the impact of any potential biases in the data that could be introduced due to under representation of certain skin conditions or demographics in the training datasets. Therefore, such platforms have the potential to greatly increase the robustness of the models to learn effective features from the data that can be generalized to cases that were not seen during the training phase.

B. Multi-Modal Frameworks

There are many other platforms currently under development that are exploring the potential of integrating AI with dermatology. One of the approaches used by many of these platforms includes the implementation of a multi-modal framework. These types of frameworks incorporate data from a variety of different sources including images, as well as dermatopathological data and patient clinical history. For example, Krishnan et al. (2025) [13] described a platform that integrated all three of these different data types into a single model, that used the information to improve the prediction of disease diagnosis. The use of multiple different sources of information to train a single model has the potential to enable the development of more accurate disease diagnosis using AI in. By combining information from a variety of different sources, the model is able to gain a more complete understanding of the data, and as such it has the potential to make more accurate predictions, and to provide more accurate diagnosis than a model that relies on a single source of information.

C. Fairness Constraints and Regulatory Approaches

Regulating AI systems for fairness is another approach to implement fair evaluations of AI systems. A common way to include fairness in the training of a machine learning model is to include fairness constraints. As Deshmukh et al. (2025) [12] suggested, including fairness constraints during the training of a model to counteract biases during evaluations of skin conditions could lead to fairer evaluations of AI systems for the diagnosis of skin conditions. This would enable the model to perform equally well for all groups of different demographic variables, such as ethnicity or skin type. This would improve the equity of using AI systems for the management of skin conditions.

D. Multidisciplinary Collaboration and Patient-Centric Systems

Innovative approaches that are currently being implemented and explored to address challenges in silico challenges as they relate to the development and implementation of AI systems for a variety of skin conditions are also discussed at length. A key approach for the development of these systems that are designed to improve diagnostic accuracy and have the ability to track a variety of different skin diseases over time, is to implement and utilize a multidisciplinary approach that would enable the collaboration of experts, including computer scientists, data ethicists, as well as health care providers. The incorporation of the diverse viewpoints of these groups of individuals would facilitate and enhance the innovative design of AI systems that are intended to have a variety of different applications in the treatment and management of a variety of different skin conditions, in order to improve their practical implementation in a dermatological setting. Such systems that are designed with input from the diverse group of individuals and experts that were previously mentioned, have been found to increase the level of acceptance of these types of systems among health care providers. This, in turn, would facilitate and improve the implementation of AI systems for a variety of different applications in the field of dermatology.

Moreover, the mentioned applications can be further enhanced by incorporating novel tools for tracking and monitoring of skin conditions over time. Therefore, the innovative systems for dermatological health care not only improve the accuracy of diagnosis but also enable on-time intervention and effective treatment of various dermatological diseases. Deshmukh and his colleagues [12] have demonstrated implementation of such a system that enabled creating of a feedback loop for patients, thereby improving health care of the patients and treatment adherence.

X. Synthesis of Challenges and Solutions

Overall, while challenges such as data quality, bias, and interdisciplinary collaboration remain significant in the deployment of AI for skin disease research, innovative approaches and solutions continue to emerge. Through synthetic data generation, multi-modal frameworks, fairness enhancements, and collaborative efforts, the landscape of AI in dermatology is gradually evolving toward systems that promise not only enhanced diagnostic accuracy but also improved patient outcomes in skin disease management., The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into dermatology has the potential to revolutionize skin disease research and clinical practice. However, the challenges identified throughout this discourse underscore the necessity for a concerted effort to address the intricacies involved in effectively harnessing AI for these purposes. The significance of maintaining high data quality, mitigating algorithm bias, and fostering interdisciplinary collaboration cannot be overstated, as these elements collectively serve as the backbone for yielding reliable and equitable AI-driven outcomes in dermatological diagnostics and treatments.

However, while significant progress is being made with the use of AI in dermatology, much work needs to be carried out to address the challenges encountered in the application of skin disease research using these systems (Sales C et al., 2025) [14]. The quality of the data used for the training of machine learning algorithms, for example, is critical in the performance of these systems. While much work has been carried out using datasets of images of skin conditions, the challenge of using these in dermatology centers around the issues of bias in the datasets used. The datasets of images of skin conditions used for the training of skin disease research using AI have been found to contain a number of biases including those that are related to the geographical location in which the images were captured as well as those that are related to the demographic of the patients in the images (Sales C et al., 2025) [14]. In order to ensure that any AI system that is used for skin disease research is of a high standard and is able to be used to diagnose a wide variety of skin conditions in all different populations, it is of paramount importance to enhance the quality of the data that is used for the training of these systems.

XI. Algorithm Bias and Equitable Healthcare Solutions

Algorithm bias presents another formidable challenge that must be addressed to promote equitable healthcare solutions. Research indicates that biased algorithms can result in inaccurate predictions for underrepresented groups, potentially exacerbating existing health disparities within dermatological care (Abbas S et al., 2025) [20]. Understanding the mechanisms that lead to such biases is essential to ensure that AI systems perform fairly across various patient demographics. Integrative approaches that utilize diverse datasets and actively engage in continuous monitoring of algorithmic performance are paramount in mitigating bias. Consequently, scholars and practitioners must remain vigilant in scrutinizing AI outcomes, ensuring that these technologies serve to enhance rather than hinder accessibility and fairness in dermatological treatment.

Moreover, the use of AI in dermatology requires an interdisciplinary approach. This means that, in addition to data scientists specializing in data, there are also dermatologists, as well as experts in public health and the social sciences, who work together to address the complex challenges that the use of this technology poses. As noted earlier, each of these fields brings its own specific knowledge and experience to the table, so that, in the end, a holistic approach can be developed and validated, and the results of AI research can be translated into practice.

XII. Future Directions and Shared Responsibility

Resolving the current challenges is essential in order to ensure that future advances in the field of AI and dermatology can be harnessed. This requires ongoing research into the complex array of issues facing users of skin analysis AI, not least that of improving the quality of the data being used to train models, whilst also identifying and seeking to minimize the many biases that currently exist in AI systems. Above all, by addressing these challenges and working towards the shared goal of developing the most robust, effective, and ethical, 'dermatology-ready' AI, stakeholders in the field of skin disease research and treatment will collectively be able to unlock the AI solution's greatest potential for the detection and treatment of a vast array of different skin diseases with the ultimate aim of delivering the highest quality of healthcare to patients of all different ages and from all around the world. In this sense, the resolution of the current array of challenges related to the use of AI in dermatology will require a shared responsibility among all stakeholders. It is in this sense that AI is bringing about a revolution in the field of healthcare, with the application of skin analysis AI in particular positioning the field of dermatology at the vanguard of this new healthcare revolution.

XIII. Conclusion

The integration of artificial intelligence into skin disease research presents both transformative opportunities and significant challenges that demand systematic attention. This review has examined the multifaceted landscape of AI applications in dermatology, highlighting critical issues surrounding data quality, algorithmic bias, and the imperative for interdisciplinary collaboration. The evidence clearly demonstrates that while AI holds immense potential for improving diagnostic accuracy, personalizing treatments, and expanding access to dermatological care worldwide, these benefits cannot be realized without addressing fundamental challenges in the development and deployment of AI systems.

Data quality remains the cornerstone challenge, with existing datasets exhibiting significant biases in demographic representation, particularly the underrepresentation of darker skin tones and diverse ethnic groups. These deficiencies directly translate into algorithmic performance disparities that can exacerbate existing health inequities rather than ameliorate them. The strategies reviewed—including data augmentation, transfer learning, standardized collection protocols, and synthetic data generation—offer viable pathways toward more robust and representative training datasets. However, technical solutions alone are insufficient without concurrent attention to the human and organizational factors that shape AI development.

Algorithmic bias, whether originating from skewed data, feature selection limitations, or developer oversight, poses a direct threat to health equity. The reviewed literature consistently demonstrates that bias in dermatological AI can lead to delayed diagnoses, inappropriate treatment recommendations, and ultimately poorer outcomes for already underserved populations. Mitigating these risks requires the integration of fairness constraints, continuous performance monitoring across demographic subgroups, and the establishment of rigorous external validation protocols that test model performance in diverse real-world settings.

Perhaps most critically, this review underscores that effective AI in dermatology cannot be developed in isolation. The collaborative engagement of dermatologists, computer scientists, data analysts, ethicists, and patients is essential for creating systems that are clinically valid, technically robust, ethically sound, and socially equitable. The examples of successful interdisciplinary partnerships reviewed herein provide templates for how such collaboration can yield models with both high accuracy and broad generalizability.

Looking forward, the field must prioritize several key objectives: the compilation of large-scale, diverse, and globally representative datasets; the development and standardization of external validation methodologies; the integration of fairness and equity metrics into model evaluation frameworks; and the establishment of regulatory guidelines that ensure AI systems meet rigorous standards for safety, efficacy, and equity before clinical deployment. Only through sustained commitment to these objectives can the dermatology community harness AI's full potential to improve skin disease diagnosis and treatment for all patients, regardless of demographic background or geographic location.
