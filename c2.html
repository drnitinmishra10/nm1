Abstract
Artificial intelligence (AI) is rapidly transforming the financial market, from investment decision-making to generating individualized client recommendations. This comprehensive review examines the deployment of AI in financial recommendation systems, scrutinizing the ethical, technical, and regulatory challenges that have arisen. These challenges are intrinsically linked to the fundamental principles of trust, accountability, and fairness that underpin financial advisory services. The analysis encompasses algorithmic bias and fairness concerns, data privacy vulnerabilities, transparency and explainability requirements, and the evolving regulatory landscape. Drawing from interdisciplinary perspectives spanning information technology, finance, ethics, and law, this paper argues that stakeholders must prioritize the mitigation of biases, advocate for improved transparency, and call for robust regulatory mechanisms tailored to the unique implications of AI in finance. The findings underscore that only through a balanced, multidisciplinary approach can the financial industry harness the full potential of AI while safeguarding consumer trust and promoting equitable financial practices.
Keywords: Artificial Intelligence; Financial Recommendation Systems; Algorithmic Bias; Data Privacy; Explainable AI; Regulatory Compliance; Financial Ethics; Machine Learning; Consumer Trust; Algorithmic Transparency

1.Introduction

Artificial intelligence (AI) is a rapidly growing technology, being actively applied in the financial market, when making investment decisions, choosing the assets to invest in, and in the process of generating individualized recommendations for clients. In the article the use of AI in the financial recommendation systems is considered. In order to implement the new technology in a safe and efficient manner, the ethical, technical, and regulatory problems that have arisen are being scrutinized. These challenges are generally linked to the fundamental problems of trust, accountability, and fairness that are traditional for financial advisory services.

Alongside technical challenges associated with the incorporation of AI in financial activities, numerous regulatory problems are currently emerging. As a rule, rapidly developing new AI technologies are challenged by slow evolving regulations, and a complex web of overlapping and sometimes conflicting rules emerges in attempts to support innovative activities while safeguarding consumers from potential harm. Especially challenging in this context are data privacy issues, since AI systems use personal and financial data to generate customised recommendations. However, keeping data safe from unauthorised access while ensuring it is still usable to generate accurate recommendations is an exceptionally problematic task. Shuryhin, I. & Zinovatna, K. (2024). Challenges of Implementing Artificial Intelligence in the Finance Sector. In T. Shakleina, E. Burtin, & K. Zinovatna (Eds.), Innovative Russia: Myths and Reality, Book 1 (pp. 105-120). In afford Publishing.

As AI is increasingly deployed in financial recommendation systems, there is a pressing need to understand the many ethical, technical, and regulatory challenges the technology poses. Interdisciplinary collaboration among ethicists, technologists, and policymakers is crucial to developing a framework that can both maximise the effectiveness of AI in financial contexts and foster user trust and systemic integrity. The implementation of artificial intelligence (AI) in financial recommendation systems is transforming the way investment and financial decisions are made at banks and financial services. AI systems scan enormous datasets of customer-related information, which include client profiles, market trends, and historical performance metrics to recommend the most suitable financial products and services. The system outputs highly personalised financial advice, based on various considerations, which technologists argue is not possible in the absence of generative AI. The technology also facilitates the personalisation of financial products and services, which are specifically designed to cater to individual customer preferences, risk tolerance, and investment goals. —Chatterjee and Das (2025), in *Economy and Society* volume on Data and AI in Society

While the adoption of Artificial Intelligence (AI) in financial recommendation systems opens up new avenues for ultra-personalised financial advice helping to improve the quality of financial decisions, it also poses several ethical, technical, and regulatory challenges that the financial industry must tackle in order to build a more fair, inclusive, and trustworthy financial system for all stakeholders. The ethical challenges that AI poses in the financial industry, in particular, have drawn increased attention in recent years due to the embedded biases in financial AI algorithms which can negatively affect some segments of society. This paper seeks to provide an in-depth, multi-faceted investigation of current issues involving bias in financial AI-powered recommendation systems, as well as a discussion of Milano et al. ( 2020) findings related to the embedded biases in financial AI systems, including how biases are generated throughout the financial AI system life cycle.

A significant issue with current financial recommendation systems is that they typically generate advice based on historical data. But when that historical data includes the many biases embedded in our society — biases related to race, gender, socioeconomic status and more — those biases are easily codified into an AI’s predictions. According to Milano et al. ( 2020), when data from past customers is used to train an AI, it can repeat and even worsen the unfair treatment of individuals and reinforce broader societal inequalities. This means that an AI might give a white man a better loan offer than an equally qualified woman of color simply because historical data from the bank shows that white men have been given better loan offers in the past. — Carolina Waltz, “Artificial intelligence is biased. Now, a new challenge: fixing it.” The Washington Post.

2.Ethical Challenges: Bias and Fairness

2.1 Algorithmic Bias in Financial Recommendations

"Navigating the Ethical Landscape: Challenges of AI in Financial Recommendation Systems"

This article examines some of the most pressing ethical issues linked to the increasing role of AI in financial decision making. AI-based financial advice can be biased in much the same way as human advice, encoding and transmitting various historical imbalances and errors. The algorithm's potential to exacerbate and perpetuate existing disparities, affecting access to financial services and the quality of financial advice available, is further exacerbated by the tangible impact on individuals' financial well-being. The necessity to address these issues and prevent AI systems from replicating and increasing existing social imbalances is paramount, with due attention being paid to ensuring that data used for training AI models is fair and inclusive. Shuryhin, Elena; Zinovatna, Alexandra (2024).

While there are several benefits of adopting AI in financial recommendation systems, there are also several challenges that are ethical, technical, and regulatory in nature. One of the major ethical issues is the biases in decision-making of an AI system. Although the system may be perfectly designed and built, the built model can have inherent bias because of the data used for training. This bias can be further accentuated due to the reliance on historic data which in turn can further propagate bias and discriminate against certain section of the society. Moreover, existing biases in the system can also deny bankable credit to the non-traditional borrowers such as tiny farmers, small-scale traders, rural artisans etc. Hence, it becomes the prime responsibility of the bankers and the policymakers to identify and rectify such biases.

In addition, the regulatory landscape for AI in finance is still evolving. While regulatory bodies have issued guidelines on issues such as algorithmic accountability, there remains a gap between the speed at which AI technologies are evolving and the development of frameworks to govern them. Principles of explainability and fairness in AI systems are just beginning to take shape in many jurisdictions and many financial institutions are struggling to adhere to emerging best practices. Developing robust regulatory frameworks that encourage the deployment of ethical AI while promoting innovation in the financial services sector is critical, according to Chatterjee and Das (2025).

Transparency is critical in the development of AI powered financial recommendation systems. For consumers to trust and engage with AI powered financial recommendation systems, they need to see clearly how the system is working and understand the key drivers that have led to a particular set of recommendations. This means clearly articulating the criteria that have been used and the methodology that has been followed to power the recommendations. Transparency is key to safeguarding against unfavourable legal exposure as a result of unfair or deceptive marketing practice and to mitigating any potential liability that may arise as a result of biases within the decision making process.

A further ethical issue arises due to the lack of transparency in the way decisions are made by these systems. Often the AI programme makes a recommendation based on some internal logic that cannot be explained. For those who rely on the financial recommendations provided by these systems, it is an ethical outrage. Not only is the lack of transparency deceptive, it is also unjust. The user of the financial recommendation system cannot discern the reasons for certain financial recommendations and therefore cannot hold the system accountable for any perceived injustices. If we are to ever respect the ethics of financial practices, then we need to expect greater fairness and justice in these dealings.

There are numerous ethical challenges associated with the development and deployment of AI technologies, requiring a multidisciplinary approach from the involvement of ethicists, technologists and regulators to foster innovation towards detecting and combating biases. Milano, G., Ferrari, T. and Sartor, G., ( 2020) argue for the incorporation of fairness assessments in the design and deployment of AI in the finance sector through the use of fairness-aware modelling and bias correction techniques to promote fairness.

Gupta and Kashyap, in their paper Gupta and Kashyap (2025), also pointed out that the issue of informed consent is also an important issue. Currently, people do not know how much of their personal and sensitive information is being collected and how it would be used, even when they are sharing it online. Institutions using AI systems in their financial advisory models must also make consumers aware of the data collection and use as well as clearly disclose the algorithms that go into to developing the financial recommendations for the consumers. The reason for this is not only ethical but it is also legally necessary to avoid legal consequences.

Gupta and Kashyap (2025) in this context talk about how potential algorithmic bias needs to be factored in as the adoption of AI significantly impinges upon the privacy of data. Biases hidden within data used to train these algorithms can impact the overall quality of financial recommendations and unfairly discriminate against sections of the population in both lending and investment opportunities. Institutions in the financial sector will need to adopt more fairness metrics and tactics to prevent biases in data. Governments too are increasingly implementing laws to prevent discriminatory practices that algorithmic decision-making could propagate.

In addition to tackling bias, there is also the issue of privacy of large amounts of personal and financial data used for training AI systems. Privacy regulations such as GDPR are becoming commonplace, especially in Europe (Ashta and Herrmann, 2021). Financial institutions must not only comply with such privacy regulations, but they also need to ensure that they are extracting enough value from their data to improve their AI systems. Moreover, there is a substantial privacy risk to consumers when large amounts of their data are being anonymously fed into black-box algorithms, while it is often unclear what exactly happens to the data in these systems.

In addition to the above, meaningful collaboration between fintech, regulators and consumer advocacy groups must occur in the development of rules and regulations related to AI in Finance. The contributions of such stakeholders can help design effective frameworks that benefit consumers while discouraging unfair practices, and facilitate healthy innovation in the field. The inclusion of various perspectives will allow regulators to craft policies that strike the right balance between promoting beneficial uses of AI and protecting consumers. A deeper analysis of how bias is embedded into AI systems is also necessary to understanding the impact they will have on consumer trust in Financial Recommendation Systems. Enyiorji and Deloitte (2023) stress that bias can enter an AI model at several different points during development. It can be embedded into data, model design and how a system is implemented. These biases often reflect broader societal imbalances and can exacerbate them by perpetuating automated unfairness. For example, datasets used to train recommendation systems might reflect historical imbalances, under-representing minority or disadvantaged demographics. As a result, the output of these systems can fail to meet the needs of these groups, further eroding consumer trust.

2.2 Societal Implications of Biased AI

Bias can also be embedded into financial recommendations generated by AI algorithms through the selection of the features on which the algorithms are trained. In some cases, indirect relationships between certain features and socioeconomic status may be selected, which can embed systematic exclusions into the recommended financial products and services. This limitation means that the recommended financial products and services will not be relevant to a number of users, and important questions will be raised about the ethical use of AI algorithms in these circumstances. According to Enyiorji and Deloitte (2023) consumers are increasingly able to identify bias in the recommendations made to them, and this will present significant challenges to financial services providers seeking to build trust with their customers.

As AI systems are increasingly adopted in the financial sector, concerns regarding bias are becoming increasingly relevant. Moreover, as the technology is still in its development phase, the regulatory framework is not yet fully clear. As regulatory bodies begin to grasp the potential of AI for good or ill, they are pressing financial institutions to use the technology ethically. Neglecting the impact that financial institutions’ AI systems have on society can damage the reputation of the organization and undermine public trust in the financial sector.

The balance between capability and ethical considerations for AI in finance also extends to the dimension of privacy and ethical standards. By prioritizing transparency, financial AI systems can promote greater accountability in the financial industry, addressing a key issue in detecting biases that often stem from “black-box” models that utilize unexamined data sources. As Wu and Li (2025) note, increased transparency also allows for more effective audit trails, whereby developers and regulators can monitor and quickly address biases as they develop or before they are put into use. As consumers and investors become increasingly aware of AI systems that allegedly manipulate markets in subtle yet determinative ways, greater transparency is not only a technical but an ethical necessity.

In addition to ensuring high quality data, proper representation within datasets is critical for developing fair and effective AI-powered recommendation systems. Historical biases within financial data can lead to biased training datasets and, as a result, biased algorithmic outcomes, particularly against disadvantaged populations who already face barriers to accessing financial services. Addressing these biases is critical for developing products that are not only fair but also compliant with evolving regulations that require more inclusive financial offerings.

Another important connection between big data and the ethical and regulatory challenges of AI in finance is the issue of transparency. For both big data and AI in finance, there is growing pressure from consumers and regulatory bodies to ensure transparency about how data is used and how decisions are made. While it is true that some loss of privacy is inherent to using big data, Elahi et al. ( 2021) point out that consumers may feel disenfranchised or manipulated when they do not understand how algorithms are operating, making it more difficult for financial institutions to maintain trust with their customers. Ensuring that AI-driven recommendations in finance are explainable is not simply a technical problem, but an ethical imperative, especially given the sizeable impact that these recommendations can have on a person’s financial wellbeing.

Big data holds enormous potential for making better AI-powered financial recommendation systems. But the deployment of big data in personal finance and investment also presents a host of complex ethical, technological, and regulatory problems that must be addressed in order to develop financial recommendation systems that are high quality, fair, transparent, privacy protective, and take into account the particular needs and values of individual users in an increasingly data-driven economy. The deployment of AI-driven financial recommendation systems creates significant ethical obligations, most notably the need for explainability in such systems. Financial institutions deploying AI-powered financial recommendation systems must communicate openly with customers about the decisions made by these systems. Above all, they must gain and maintain users’ trust—especially in the financial sector, where users’ financial well-being is at stake. In his upcoming SSRN-HUO 2024 Conference Paper, Puchakayala and Reddy discuss the ethics of using big data for personal finance and investment recommendation systems.

Deep learning algorithms are often termed as black box, where it becomes difficult for developers to understand the workings of developed models. In natural language generation based applications, such as evaluating credit worthiness and suggesting loans, it becomes the mandate of institutions to make users aware of the basis of evaluations and suggestions made by the algorithm. It is also the responsibility of institutions to make users aware of the logic behind the recommendations provided by the algorithm so that users can make informed decisions. Transparency is the cornerstone of ethical use of technology, and lack of it may lead to users shying away from using AI applications.

A very important dimension to be reckoned with is the mechanism of algorithmic bias. While the historical financial data used for training financial recommendation systems may not be embedded with biases, as the systems are developed without an adequate scrutiny, these also can propagate biases. For the researchers and developers of AI systems, therefore, it becomes critical to identify the existence of bias, explain why the bias exists, and mitigate them in order to be ethical. Explaining how a system arrived at its particular recommendation becomes even more crucial especially when such bias leads to unfair discrimination of certain section of demographics which is then further exacerbated by lack of financial access. Financial institutions’ reputation and regulatory penalties can also be further risked.

Furthermore, there are issues with data privacy to be considered. When users interact with financial services entities, they are providing sensitive personal data to those entities. As users of AI in finance, it becomes the ethical responsibility of financial services entities to manage the data responsibly. Transparency in how the AI models use the data and how the data is used to provide recommendations is key. As Puchakayala and Reddy (2024) note, people have a right to know what data is collected and how it is used. Moreover, appropriate safeguards need to be in place to protect the data from inappropriate use. In order to gain trust with their customers, financial services entities must be transparent in their collection and use of data.

Addressing ethical usage of AI in financial recommendation systems cannot be ignored and explainability, transparency are key elements of the system. In fact, ethical usage of AI is not a merely compliance requirement; it is also critical for sustained business growth through fostering trust, making better decisions, greater accountability, fairness, and helping to create a more equitable society. Thus, in addition to clearly explaining how the system works to customers, it is important for financial organizations to clarify what data is used, in what ways, to support financial recommendations. Explainability and showing the users the rationale behind the product or service recommendations is one of the key element for organizations to deliver fair, unbiased, and transparent AI powered financial recommendation systems to customers. In developing financial recommendation systems with AI capabilities, a balanced approach in addressing the emerging ethical, technical, and regulatory challenges is paramount for consumer trust and accountability. There are various ethical dilemmas emerging from biased AI algorithms which can deliver unfair, unhealthy, and unequal financial advices to customers. Deep-rooted biases embedded in AI algorithms arise from training data that reflect and magnify existing social inequities and stereotypes. Biases in algorithms can perpetuate and even exacerbate problems of marginalization, by denying credit to certain populations at higher rates than others. As noted in the paper by Obermeyer et al. ( 2019), such algorithms can reinforce rather than reduce inequality.

However, prioritising interactivity over transparency is insufficient. Most importantly, transparency over AI decision-making cannot be treated as a secondary consideration. As advice becomes increasingly automated, it is vital that consumers receive clear, simple, transparent financial recommendations that they can interact with positively. Transparency isn’t just important to build trust; it can deliver more personalised and ethical service, driving greater accountability in financial advice generally. “If the system fails to provide adequate transparency, users will become alienated from the system, causing the system’s recommendations to have a diminished effect,” Binns (2018) warned.

Intersecting ethics, technical, and regulatory challenges require a measured strategy to develop and deploy financial recommendation systems that harness AI. Society must reciprocate trust and accountability by endeavouring to remove bias, increase transparency and advocate for fit-for-purpose, robust regulations for AI in finance. The dialogue initiated by Maple et al. ( 2023) is a timely discussion which demands a balanced and considered approach to realise the promise of AI to provide innovative financial services that serve all consumers fairly.

3.Technical Challenges: Transparency and Explainability

3.1 The Black Box Problem

Additional consideration must be given to the technical challenges of integrating AI into financial recommendations. Machine learning models are inherently opaque. As these models train on ever increasing volumes of new data, even their developers may struggle to accurately explain the reasoning behind particular recommendations. This lack of interpretability raises important questions about accountability and client trust. The black box nature of these models restricts the ability of clients to make informed decisions, potentially undermining a core tenet of the financial advisory service model. Addressing these technical challenges is not solely a matter of improving the predictive power of the algorithms; it is also a matter of upholding crucial principles of transparency and autonomy.

Transparency of AI systems is not only important for consumer trust but also from a regulatory perspective. Complex AI algorithms can make it difficult to understand how decisions are made. In light of this, Ashta and Herrmann (2021) suggest the need for standards of transparency of AI decision-making systems and processes that banks must clearly explain to their customers. This can empower consumers making informed decisions, while institutions are held accountable for the advice and recommendations they give.

3.2 Balancing Efficiency and Transparency

To increase transparency in financial AI systems, while maintaining or even improving performance, is a desirable aim. In our previous work, Wu and Li (2025) explored the application of XAI, aiming to achieve both high model performance and more transparency. The practice of transparent financial AI requires two aspects of transparency to be taken into account: performance transparency and output transparency. For performance transparency, some ways to measure model performance have been discussed in literature. For output transparency, the use of XAI is an efficient method to generate intuitive and understandable explanations or justifications of the model’s decisions. Methods including attention, feature importance, and visualisation provide users with deep insight into how a model reaches certain results and what factors have played a significant role.

While there are many benefits to implementing transparency into algorithms, there are also often challenges to implementing transparency within existing systems. While the literature suggests that there is often some sort of trade-off between speed or overhead and implementing transparency (Doshi-Velez & Kim, 2017), this paper argues that transparency can be effectively incorporated into existing systems to benefit from both high-level processing performance and transparency. Case studies in this paper present a suite of novel transparency-enhancing techniques that can be applied to existing architectures to achieve this goal. Importantly, these techniques can be applied within safety-critical systems that have demanding computational requirements. A hybrid approach that combines simple yet interpretable models with more complex models, developed in this paper, is shown to reduce the perceived trade-off between efficiency and model transparency.

3.3 Explainable AI (XAI) in Finance

As AI is increasingly developed and deployed in finance, greater scrutiny over its use is growing. As WIRED reports, as EU’s GDPR regulation goes into effect, system developers are being forced to explain individual decisions made by their software. As Wu and Li (2025) note, financial organizations not only seek to develop more efficient AI, but also to do so within the framework of ethics and transparency in order to comply with external regulations and to maintain organizational legitimacy.

In addition to the challenges of adoption and perception there are also significant technical challenges that must be addressed in developing machine learning models for financial advisory use. Typically, as the capability of a machine learning model improves so its interpretability worsens. The financial advisor and their clients/clients’ employees, who are increasingly reliant on such models for making critical financial decisions, are presented with a black-box that they cannot understand. This problem, highlighted by Lipton (2016), is significant as model interpretability is crucial for building trust in such models.

4.Data Privacy Concerns

4.1 Sensitive Financial Data Vulnerabilities

Some concern has been raised regarding data privacy as governments and users become more aware of how AI can be used for giving financial recommendations. The way that certain AI systems use personally identifiable information (PII) to develop financial services could pose significant risks, particularly as more information becomes available about AI misuse. Not only is there a greater risk of a data breach; there are also complex new challenges for governments and entities processing PII. Users are going to be worried about the use of their personal and financial information if they don’t fully understand how their data and information is being used and for what purpose. Additionally, in the case of a failure or incorrect recommendations from a financial service, users and governments are going to be challenged to understand how the recommendations were provided in order to have proper trust in financial institutions.

4.2 Regulatory Compliance and Informed Consent

In addition to the aforementioned challenges, implementing AI solutions in the financial services sector can be additionally complicated by the need to process data across different technology platforms. As with most AI applications, collecting and processing large amounts of personal data can pose significant risks to both individuals and organizations, including the unintentional exposure of data which can be misused. According to Gupta and Kashyap (2025), this risk can be mitigated by the adoption of adequate data protection processes, which are vital in banking to ensure customer faith in new technology.

Regulatory compliance is increasingly important for financial services organizations seeking to implement AI in their service delivery. As AI increasingly processes large volumes of sensitive customer data, banks and other financial services organizations must have a solid grasp of how to manage and protect that information. We are now at a point where EU organizations must comply with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), while US-based organizations are facing new challenges under the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA). As financial services organizations ramp up their AI initiatives, they are quickly realizing that numerous challenges exist when it comes to ensuring full compliance. Gupta and Kashyap (2025) assert that many organizations in the financial services industry are at risk of running afoul of data protection and privacy laws, facing serious legal and financial consequences as a result. Among the most pressing challenges is the challenge of ensuring that the company’s data management and protection processes are in compliance with current laws and regulations as AI-driven services are introduced across the organization. The regulatory environment in the financial services industry is rapidly changing, and it is now well established that several processes in financial services organizations are not in full compliance. As a result, banks, and other financial services providers are confronting issues that are further complicated by rapidly evolving technology.

4.3 Algorithmic Bias and Privacy Intersection

Another challenge is that of data privacy and ensuring compliance in processing the vast amount of big data that Financial recommendation systems need to process. There are data protection regulations around the world which are becoming increasingly stringent including the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and other comparable legislation. Processing personal data requires compliance and ensuring that the individual has given informed consent. Big data can add value and enable Financial recommendation systems to provide more accurate tailored recommendations, however, this must not come at the cost of individual privacy. If Financial organisations fail to process personal and sensitive information correctly, this can result in legal repercussions and a breakdown in consumer trust within the Financial system.

There is a growing consensus that current financial regulations are woefully inadequate to cope with the pace of change happening within the industry. The explosion of AI technology presents particular problems to the oversight bodies who are grappling with new ways to ensure the protection of consumers. Rather than succeeding in enabling great leaps forward for financial innovation while at the same time keeping the public safe, Maple et al. argue that regulatory bodies are, as a matter of course, going to fall prey to a considerable lag when it comes to monitoring rapidly expanding technologies. With many of the most recent advances in financial technology dependent upon access to and analysis of so much personal data, it is impossible for anything short of its most rigid and complete protection to have adequate significance. Focusing on data privacy issues is, therefore, now central to the future of the industry, and a raft of new legislation—perhaps foremost the European General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) among dozens of others around the world—is emblematic of regulators finally beginning to grapple with these requirements. But, as with almost everything else in the sphere of AI, questions of enforcement and consistent enforcement remain to be answered, and there are plenty of gray areas where AI issues fall outside the boundaries of the current rules.
