Abstract

The integration of Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) into dermatology is transforming skin disease diagnosis and treatment, offering greater accuracy and individualized approaches. Deep learning-based models, which have achieved state-of-the-art results in several health applications, are often reported as 'black boxes' as their decision processes are difficult to interpret. Therefore, in health applications, including dermatology where images are used for diagnosis, the use of such models has generated significant concern among both health care professionals and patients. This paper reviews the current state of the art of Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) in dermatology, as well as the opportunities and challenges related to its clinical implementation. We examine how XAI methods facilitate the development of transparent models, improve diagnostic accuracy, enhance clinical decision support, and address ethical considerations including data privacy, algorithmic bias, and accountability. Furthermore, we identify key challenges such as data quality requirements, model interpretability trade-offs, computational resource demands, and the need for interdisciplinary collaboration. The findings suggest that while XAI holds tremendous potential for dermatological care, robust ethical frameworks, comprehensive training protocols, and scalable implementation strategies are essential for successful clinical integration.

Index Terms—Explainable artificial intelligence, dermatology, deep learning, skin cancer diagnosis, clinical decision support, algorithmic bias, medical AI ethics

I. Introduction

The integration of Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) into dermatology is changing skin disease diagnosis and treatment, offering greater accuracy and individualized approaches. An important goal of current health technologies is the improvement of diagnosis and related clinical decisions. Deep learning-based models, which have achieved state-of-the-art results in several health applications, are often reported as 'black boxes' as their decision processes are difficult to interpret. Therefore, in health applications, including dermatology where images are used for diagnosis, the use of such models has generated significant concern among both health care professionals and patients.

Health care professionals require greater trust in the health information provided by AI systems to integrate them into their decision-making processes. Moreover, greater trust in health information is required from patients in order to increase their levels of adherence to recommended treatments and to improve their health outcomes. Therefore, XAI methods are important for dermatological classification systems, as they facilitate the development of transparent models. In this work, the current state of the art of Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) in dermatology, as well as the opportunities and challenges related to its use, are reviewed.

II. Diagnostic Accuracy and Clinical Decision Support

A. Improved Diagnostic Accuracy

The key advantage of XAI in the context of skin cancer diagnosis is the potential for improved diagnostic accuracy. Currently, skin cancer is diagnosed with high accuracy using clinical images by human dermatologists. Machine learning models that are trained on large datasets can make decisions using patterns in the data that may be beyond the human doctor's ability to recognize. Through XAI methods, the decisions of such machine learning models can be analyzed and better understood.

For instance, a model could be used to analyze images of suspicious skin lesions to identify those that are likely to be malignant melanoma. The model could provide an explanation of why a particular lesion was classified as a melanoma. For example, the model may have focused on features such as the unusual texture or color of the lesion. This type of information could be used by a dermatologist to validate the decision made by the model (Haenssle et al., 2018).

B. Enhanced Decision Support and Shared Decision Making

Explainable AI can be implemented in dermatology in order to enhance decision support. It can analyze the huge amount of data stored in large databases and provide assistance in treating skin diseases in an individualized manner. In dermatology, many different treatment options are available for each skin condition, and with the help of AI, different treatment options can be analyzed for each individual patient. With the help of Explainable AI, the reasons for a chosen treatment can be explained to the patient, which can increase compliance and patient satisfaction. A study published in 2020 by Tschandl et al. emphasizes the importance of shared decision making in dermatology and how AI can support this process.

III. Challenges in Clinical Implementation

A. Data Quality and Bias

Several challenges for the clinical implementation of XAI methods in dermatology must be addressed. An important challenge is the requirement for large and high-quality datasets to train AI models. The bias introduced by underrepresented skin conditions in the large amount of already available dermatological datasets (Fitzgerald et al., 2021) might even be increased by the application of XAI methods. A further challenge for the clinical implementation of XAI methods in dermatology is the required additional training for explanation interpretation by dermatologists. As the benefit of XAI methods can only be achieved if AI decisions are sufficiently understood by clinicians, it must be ensured that XAI models are explained well enough by AI systems to be sufficiently interpreted by users.

B. Accountability and Privacy

When integrating AI methods into the practice of dermatology for the diagnosis of skin diseases, important accountability issues must be addressed. For example, how do we hold accountable those who program an AI system that suggests an inaccurate diagnosis, the clinicians who relied on the system for a diagnosis, or the institution where the mistake occurred? All three of these individuals or groups may be accountable in different ways than before when dermatologists practiced using clinical expertise for their diagnoses.

Of special concern when implementing XAI for dermatological diagnoses and their associated treatment recommendations are the privacy rights of patients, as XAI algorithms rely on a substantial amount of individual health data, information that is generally considered to be highly sensitive (American Medical Association, 2021). We have a responsibility as researchers and practitioners to safeguard the rights of patients in terms of ensuring their health information is kept confidential and ensuring that any data collected and/or shared for the training and validation of XAI has been de-identified to protect against potential privacy breaches.

IV. Technical Approaches and Model Interpretability

A. Transfer Learning and Fine-Tuning

Implementing Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) in the classification of skin diseases can be improved by using XAI to enhance diagnostic accuracy and treatment recommendations. The interpretability of deep learning models in healthcare classification, such as in dermatology, can be improved by using XAI, therefore enabling their implementation by clinicians in their daily practice. For instance, transfer learning between deep learning models trained on different classification datasets can be utilized to improve the performance of a model on a given dataset. Moreover, models trained on huge datasets of images of skin lesions can be fine-tuned using small datasets to improve the performance of the model on a specific classification task. For instance, Abbas et al. (2025) used a pre-trained convolutional neural network (CNN), pre-trained on the ImageNet dataset, that was fine-tuned on two different datasets of images of skin lesions for the classification of two different types of skin diseases.

B. Grad-CAM and Localization Techniques

The local areas of images that are relevant for classification, i.e., the areas corresponding to the features used by the model for classification, can be highlighted using XAI. For instance, Grad-CAM (Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping), a technique used for highlighting relevant parts of images using CNNs, can be used to highlight the local areas of images of skin lesions that were used by a deep learning model for classification. These areas may correspond to features such as patches, lesions, nails, hair, etc. Therefore, using XAI can be improved by combining different techniques for improving the interpretability of deep learning models. Moreover, using XAI in conjunction with other techniques for improving model performance, such as ensemble methods, bagging, and boosting, can also improve model performance.

C. Ensemble Methods and Model Collaboration

XAI can also be used for highlighting local areas of images relevant for making decisions using deep learning models that highlight parts of images relevant for classification. For instance, Tschandl et al. (2020) used a CNN called DermoAI that was able to highlight the local areas of images of skin lesions used by the model for classification, in order to help clinicians make decisions. The images highlighted by the model using DermoAI showed the features of the images used for classification, such as patches, lesions, nails, and hair. Moreover, the features highlighted by the model using DermoAI were also annotated by the model to help clinicians make decisions. Therefore, the implementation of XAI in the classification of skin diseases using deep learning models can highlight the local areas of images of skin lesions relevant for classification, in addition to improving model interpretability.

Interactions and discussions between dermatologists and XAI-based systems support a more rational use of algorithms and enable a clearer explanation of how the model arrived at a certain diagnosis. Furthermore, techniques such as Grad-CAM enable the localization of regions of interest on images and support the clinician in their diagnostics. This diagnostic process increases confidence in the correct use of AI in order to support the patient with the best treatment. For this reason, model explainability supports efficient collaboration between dermatologists and algorithms.

V. Clinical Outcomes and Healthcare Efficiency

The use of XAI in the classification of skin diseases leads to improved diagnostics in terms of accuracy and clinical decision-making support. This translates into improved clinical outcomes, for example in terms of reduced numbers of errors, speed of diagnosis of critical diseases, and improved treatment outcomes through more individualized treatment. Skin diseases can be identified and treated more effectively with the aid of XAI methods in conjunction with large-scale Electronic Health Record (EHR) systems for predictive analytics. For example, the EHR system can identify patients at risk of developing certain diseases earlier than clinical diagnosis, allowing interventions to prevent serious morbidity and mortality.

Beyond the improved efficiency of clinicians and improved health outcomes of patients, XAI methods for skin diseases can also fulfill an important function in relation to current requirements for transparency of health care technologies. It is well known that AI applications are increasingly being scrutinized. The aspect of explainability of decisions made by a machine plays a critical role in health care providers' acceptance of these technologies, as well as in that of their patients. In dermatological diagnosis and treatment, XAI can be used to provide explanations for decisions made by a computer for the clinical diagnosis and treatment of a patient's skin lesions. Providing explanations for decisions of a computer system used in health care not only leads to improved efficiency of health care providers and improved health outcomes of patients, but also enables informed consent and shared decision making in health care, which is the ethical foundation of clinical practice. Thus, a patient who has been treated for a skin lesion, by gaining insight into the reasons for a particular chosen treatment, can be motivated to participate more actively in his or her health care, which can result in better health outcomes.

VI. Bias, Generalizability, and Population Diversity

While these tools have tremendous potential, the complex nature of certain skin conditions and the vast array of the human population and their varied skin types will introduce inherent biases. To increase the generalizability of performance across diverse populations, models will have to be tweaked or fine-tuned for individual applications. Much of the responsibility for using these tools accurately will rest on the AI developer, but also on the clinician using the model. Thus, the clinician will also have to receive adequate training on how to implement these tools as part of their practice.

While XAI brings several benefits for dermatology in terms of increased accuracy in diagnosing various skin diseases, improving patient outcomes, and helping to treat patients with personalized treatments, many challenges must first be overcome by developers of AI systems for the dermatological field. This is especially the case when looking at the data used to train models for various applications, with many failing to meet the high standards required. The samples of data used to train AI models must be of the highest quality in order to create models able to make accurate diagnoses across a wide range of patients from diverse backgrounds.

As previously mentioned, there are a number of skin conditions that can present themselves differently on the skin of people from different races and ethnicities (Singh et al., 2024). This means that there is a real risk that the data used to train an AI model could contain biases that could affect the way the model makes diagnoses. These biases could have negative effects, including failure to diagnose various skin conditions in patients from certain backgrounds, leading to failure to provide the best possible treatment. It is therefore of paramount importance that the data used to train AI models for dermatology is of the highest possible quality to ensure accurate diagnoses across a wide range of skin diseases in patients from diverse backgrounds.

VII. Interpretability vs. Accuracy Trade-off

Interpretability of models represents significant challenges to implementing explainable AI in dermatology, although such methods can facilitate increased transparency of the diagnostic process for the practitioner. Currently available methods for implementing XAI are not always sufficiently transparent or user-friendly for application in a dermatological setting. There exists a significant trade-off between a model's ability to make accurate predictions and its ability to be explained, as exemplified by the results of a recent study that presented a novel method for increasing interpretability in deep neural networks for image classification (Nasir et al., 2024). The study noted that while the method significantly improved the ability of a deep neural network to be explained, there was a marked decrease in the network's ability to make accurate predictions. The challenge, therefore, is to develop methods of increasing interpretability that do not decrease the ability to make accurate predictions and that are easy enough to implement that users can be confident they have a thorough understanding of the model.

VIII. Computational Requirements and Resource Accessibility

In addition, a large amount of computational power is needed to train complex AI models. These computational needs require state-of-the-art computer hardware as well as highly trained IT professionals to implement, manage, and debug complex AI systems. The challenge of computational needs of XAI systems is that they are often beyond the capacity of lower-resourced dermatological clinics to implement. Consequently, there is a risk of increased healthcare disparity for patients in lower-resourced clinics who may not have access to dermatological care with the assistance of the latest XAI technologies (Singh et al., 2024). In other words, the best AI models in dermatology may only be used in well-funded clinics with ample IT support to manage and implement these complex AI models. Scalable XAI models with minimal computational power requirements are sorely needed to reduce IT barriers to the implementation of the best AI methods in dermatology and to improve access to cutting-edge dermatological care for all.

IX. Ethical Considerations

A. Data Privacy and Governance

One of the many ethical concerns with the use of XAI in dermatology for the diagnosis of skin diseases is data privacy. With more healthcare data being collected and analyzed by AI systems, the risk of compromising patients' health information increases. All healthcare information collected by AI systems must be kept confidential to avoid any abuse. The ethical principle of beneficence requires that healthcare providers protect their patients' health information above all else. Implementing necessary data protection and transparent use of health information by AI systems is of great importance to healthcare providers. Such measures must be in place to safeguard patients' health information and protect their right to privacy. Alkhanbouli and colleagues (2025) explained that developing and establishing guidelines for data governance is paramount to addressing and mitigating risks to patients' health information, and in turn, to building patients' and public trust in healthcare systems.

B. Informed Consent and Patient Understanding

Informed consent is another very important consideration when implementing XAI systems in dermatology. Due to the complexity of these algorithms, there is concern that even health care providers may not be able to fully understand the outputs and decisions of AI systems. Yet, patients have a right to be fully informed of the methods used to treat them, including the use of AI in health care. This means that XAI systems must be designed so that health care providers are able to explain the decisions of the system to their patients, and that patients are able to understand these explanations. This can be particularly challenging for complex algorithms, and requires significant effort to make patients fully aware of the limitations of the system and how bias can be introduced. Patient involvement in discussions of the role of AI in health care can be very powerful in increasing patient engagement and ensuring that the patient is fully involved in health care decisions.

C. Algorithmic Bias and Health Equity

Bias in AI is a further ethical issue as it can cause inequality in terms of results and subsequent treatment provided to patients. There are a number of sources that could result in AI bias. One key source is the data that the AI has been trained with. There is a risk of this data not being representative of the patient demographics seen by the dermatologist. For example, the dermatoscopic images used in skin analysis by AI could be of Caucasian skin type, which would cause problems for people of different skin types as their images would not be recognized by the AI, leading to misdiagnosis. This would mean that the AI is being used to discriminate against certain groups of people, which is unethical and should be avoided. Clinicians and developers of XAI must examine the data that the model has been trained with and check whether it is representative of all patient demographics. They must also ensure that the model has been validated on different demographic groups to check for bias in the model's decisions.

D. Clinical Authority and Professional Responsibility

Moreover, incorporating XAI in clinical workflows requires revisiting longstanding medical hierarchies. While doctors and other healthcare workers benefit from having AI perform diagnostic functions and process images, the system can also subtly decrease the authority and importance of human physicians. Because the AI is accountable for the analysis it conducts, decisions made with the help of XAI algorithms could potentially lead to complex problems when they result in inaccurate or harmful diagnostic determinations. In such cases, those who utilized the AI may claim that they were merely following the system's recommendations and that they did their due diligence in adhering to the algorithm's determinations. In order to protect the integrity of their practices and uphold fundamental medical ethics such as non-maleficence and patient autonomy, practitioners must maintain deep awareness of the limitations of the AI they utilize. To this end, all professionals utilizing XAI must engage in constant practice and retain deep familiarity with all the processes and judgments they make.

XII. Ongoing Challenges and Future Directions

Challenges with XAI practical application persist; clinical practitioners are hesitant to apply such tools into their practice due to complexity in data privacy issues and complexity in model interpretability. It is also a misconception that XAI could improve clinical decision-making. This must be rigorously and comprehensively tested, and its performance with respect to traditional state-of-the-art clinical decision support systems must be objectively evaluated. Studies are ongoing; numerous clinical trials have been and are being conducted in this field, aimed at providing proof for XAI-based practical applications.

As the body of evidence supporting the use of XAI in skin disease diagnosis and treatment continues to grow, the ethical and practical challenges for the implementation of these systems in the clinic need to be addressed. These challenges, and in particular the question of accountability for errors as well as potential algorithmic bias, need to be tackled by researchers, developers, and clinicians alike. They require a joint effort to set up the ethical frameworks that are needed to allow the implementation of XAI in the clinic. The key to overcoming current hurdles is to work together as a community and address the challenges that need to be solved. In this sense, the ethical considerations for the use of XAI in dermatology will need to be tackled by the developers of these systems in close collaboration with the end-users, i.e., the dermatologists. The impact that XAI will have on the clinic, and in particular on the relationship between the dermatologist and the patient, will need to be addressed, and potential changes to the current workflow of a dermatologist will need to be taken into account. In the end, it is the task of the developers of these systems to work together with the clinicians to set up the most appropriate ethical frameworks, which are needed to guarantee the accountability of the decisions that are made by XAI systems, and which are needed to guarantee that the use of these systems does not lead to potential discrimination.

Moreover, integration of different multimodal imaging techniques in a system to enhance model accuracy and increase the clinical utility of computer-assisted dermatological diagnostics and care has the potential to deliver results of highest quality in the emerging field of dermatological care and skin cancer diagnostics. This allows a physician to select the most optimal modality or combination of different modalities for analysis of dermoscopic images and histopathological images of skin lesions. Utilizing XAI techniques with such emerging multimodal systems has tremendous potential to deliver highest quality dermatological care and to develop precision-, personalized-, and point-of-care-type dermatological diagnostics and care, enabling a dermatologist to select the best treatment option or set of treatment options for each individual patient on a case-by-case basis (Nguyen et al., 2024).

However, XAI solutions for dermatology are not ready to be implemented in practice yet. While they are very promising, several challenges must be overcome before they can be effectively implemented in clinical settings. First, a robust framework for data governance, ethical collection, storage, and use of patient data is required. As mentioned previously, large amounts of data are required to train XAI models. Therefore, data privacy and the potential for algorithmic bias are serious challenges that must be addressed. In particular, the representation of different skin types in the datasets used for training the models must be adequate in order to avoid inaccurate diagnosis and increasing health disparities (Benassi & Santoro, 2023).

Additionally, structured training for the use of XAI systems by dermatologists as well as other health care providers is essential. Since XAI is a developing field, it is crucial that those who use the systems have working knowledge of the strengths and limitations of the systems to ensure proper use. To that end, interdisciplinary teams of dermatologists, computer scientists, and ethicists can work together to establish best practices in the use of XAI in health care.

Accountability and transparency of XAI systems in dermatology are critical and are currently the subject of much discussion. There is a need to establish protocols regarding accountability of AI systems in dermatology. Furthermore, fostering a culture of transparency regarding the strengths and limitations of current XAI models is crucial to manage the public's and the clinicians' expectations.

XIII. Conclusion

In summary, there is tremendous scope in skin and mucosal disease diagnostics and management with incorporation of the XAI models, provided anticipated ethical challenges are addressed to develop a health care delivery model incorporating XAI with dermatological solutions at its core, keeping in mind anticipated challenges to be surmounted.

The integration of Explainable Artificial Intelligence into dermatology represents a transformative opportunity to enhance diagnostic accuracy, improve clinical workflows, and foster greater trust between patients, clinicians, and AI systems. Through techniques such as Grad-CAM, LIME, and SHAP, XAI provides the transparency necessary for clinicians to validate AI-generated recommendations and for patients to understand their diagnostic and treatment pathways. However, significant challenges remain, including the need for diverse and high-quality training data, the resolution of accuracy-interpretability trade-offs, the reduction of computational barriers for resource-limited settings, and the establishment of robust ethical frameworks addressing privacy, consent, bias, and accountability.

Successful implementation will require sustained interdisciplinary collaboration among AI researchers, dermatologists, ethicists, policymakers, and patient advocates. Only through such collaborative efforts can the full potential of XAI be realized in dermatology—delivering precision, personalized, and equitable skin care for diverse populations worldwide. Future research must prioritize clinical validation studies, the development of lightweight and scalable XAI models, and the creation of comprehensive governance structures that ensure these powerful technologies serve to enhance, rather than diminish, the human elements of medical care.
