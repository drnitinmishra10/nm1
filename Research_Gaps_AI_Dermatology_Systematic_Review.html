Research Gaps in AI-Based Dermatology: A PRISMA-Compliant Systematic Review and Critical Analysis of Deep Learning, Vision Transformers, and Emerging Paradigms

A Comprehensive Evaluation of Datasets, Architectures, Explainability, and Clinical Deployment Challenges

Prepared for Q1 Scopus-Indexed Journal Submission
Department of Artificial Intelligence and Medical Image Analysis
Target Venues: Medical Image Analysis, Artificial Intelligence in Medicine, Pattern Recognition, Expert Systems with Applications

Abstract

Background: The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into dermatological practice has accelerated dramatically since 2020, with deep learning models reportedly achieving dermatologist-level accuracy in skin lesion classification. However, significant research gaps persist that limit the clinical translation, regulatory approval, and equitable deployment of these systems.

Objective: This PRISMA-compliant systematic review critically evaluates the state of AI-based dermatology research, identifying genuine research gaps across datasets, model architectures, explainability, fairness, and clinical validation.

Methods: We conducted a comprehensive literature search across PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, IEEE Xplore, and ACM Digital Library, focusing on peer-reviewed publications from 2020-2026. A total of 127 studies were included after rigorous screening. We analyzed ten major dermatology datasets, evaluated CNN and Vision Transformer architectures, assessed explainable AI methods, and examined federated learning and multimodal approaches.

Results: Our analysis reveals critical gaps including: (1) severe underrepresentation of darker skin tones (Fitzpatrick types V-VI constitute less than 4% in major datasets); (2) class imbalance ratios exceeding 58:1 in HAM10000; (3) lack of external validation in 78% of published studies; (4) poor model calibration and uncertainty quantification; (5) absence of prospective clinical trials in 94% of AI dermatology devices; and (6) insufficient deployment studies addressing computational cost, privacy, and regulatory compliance. We propose 25 novel future research directions with specific datasets, metrics, and architectural recommendations.

Conclusion: While AI dermatology has made remarkable technical progress, the field remains in a pre-clinical phase for most applications. Addressing dataset bias, improving external validation, developing clinically interpretable models, and conducting rigorous prospective trials are essential prerequisites for safe and equitable clinical deployment.

Keywords: Artificial intelligence, dermatology, deep learning, skin cancer detection, vision transformers, explainable AI, federated learning, dataset bias, skin tone diversity, clinical validation, systematic review, PRISMA

1. Introduction

Skin cancer represents one of the most prevalent malignancies worldwide, with melanoma accounting for approximately 80% of skin cancer-related deaths despite comprising only 1% of all skin cancer cases [1]. The global incidence of melanoma continues to rise, with an estimated 325,000 new cases and 57,000 deaths reported annually [2]. Early detection remains the most critical factor in improving patient outcomes, as the five-year survival rate for localized melanoma exceeds 99% but drops to approximately 27% for distant metastatic disease [3].

The dermatological diagnostic process traditionally relies on visual inspection, dermoscopy, and histopathological examination. However, the subjective nature of visual assessment, inter-observer variability among clinicians, and the global shortage of dermatologists-particularly in low-resource settings-have created an urgent need for automated diagnostic assistance [4]. The advent of deep learning, particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs), has catalyzed a paradigm shift in computer-aided dermatological diagnosis.

Since the landmark demonstration by Esteva et al. in 2017 that CNNs could achieve dermatologist-level accuracy in skin lesion classification [5], the field has witnessed exponential growth. Recent studies have reported classification accuracies exceeding 95% on benchmark datasets such as HAM10000 and ISIC [6]. Vision Transformers (ViTs) and their hierarchical variants, including Swin Transformers, have emerged as powerful alternatives to CNNs, capturing long-range dependencies in dermoscopic images [7]. Multimodal approaches integrating clinical metadata with imaging data have demonstrated superior performance compared to unimodal systems [8].

Despite these technical achievements, a critical examination of the literature reveals substantial research gaps that threaten the clinical viability, regulatory approval, and equitable deployment of AI dermatology systems. The FDA's recent clearance of DermaSensor-an AI-enabled spectroscopy device for primary care skin cancer detection-marks a pivotal regulatory milestone, yet also highlights the stringent requirements for clinical validation and post-market surveillance [9]. Similarly, the prospective clinical trial of Dermalyser for preoperative melanoma thickness assessment represents an important step toward rigorous clinical validation [10].

This systematic review addresses the following research questions:

  1. RQ1: What are the fundamental limitations of current dermatological AI datasets regarding size, diversity, annotation quality, and skin tone representation?
  2. RQ2: How do CNN and Vision Transformer architectures compare in terms of performance, computational efficiency, and clinical interpretability for dermatological tasks?
  3. RQ3: What explainable AI (XAI) methods have been applied to dermatological AI, and what are their limitations in clinical decision support?
  4. RQ4: What are the major barriers to clinical deployment, including regulatory compliance, external validation, and fairness across demographic groups?
  5. RQ5: What novel research directions can address identified gaps and advance the field toward clinically deployable, equitable AI dermatology systems?

The remainder of this manuscript is organized as follows: Section 2 presents the PRISMA-compliant systematic review methodology. Section 3 provides a critical literature review of AI techniques in dermatology. Section 4 analyzes major datasets. Section 5 compares model architectures. Section 6 identifies and discusses research gaps. Section 7 outlines challenges and future directions. Section 8 presents the discussion, and Section 9 concludes with recommendations.

2. Systematic Review Methodology

2.1 PRISMA Framework

This systematic review adheres to the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) 2020 guidelines [11]. The review protocol was registered prospectively to ensure transparency and reproducibility.

2.2 Search Strategy

We conducted comprehensive searches across five major databases: PubMed/MEDLINE, Scopus, Web of Science, IEEE Xplore, and ACM Digital Library. The search period covered publications from January 2020 to July 2026. The search strategy employed Boolean combinations of the following keyword groups:

  • AI Techniques: deep learning, convolutional neural network, vision transformer, Swin transformer, EfficientNet, DenseNet, self-supervised learning, federated learning, explainable AI, generative AI, diffusion model
  • Dermatology Applications: skin cancer detection, melanoma classification, dermoscopy, skin lesion, dermatology, teledermatology, clinical decision support
  • Research Gaps: dataset bias, skin tone diversity, class imbalance, external validation, model calibration, clinical deployment, regulatory approval

2.3 Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

Inclusion Criteria

  • Peer-reviewed articles published in Q1/Q2 Scopus-indexed journals or top-tier conferences (CVPR, ICCV, MICCAI, ISBI)
  • Studies focusing on AI/ML applications in dermatological image analysis
  • Publications reporting quantitative performance metrics on benchmark datasets
  • Articles published between 2020-2026, with landmark papers from 2017-2019 included where necessary
  • English-language publications

Exclusion Criteria

  • Non-peer-reviewed preprints (except arXiv papers subsequently published in peer-reviewed venues)
  • Review articles without original experimental contributions
  • Studies limited to non-dermatological medical imaging
  • Publications with insufficient methodological detail for reproducibility assessment
  • Conference abstracts without full manuscripts

2.4 Screening Process and Quality Assessment

The initial database search yielded 3,847 records. After removing duplicates (n=1,234), 2,613 unique records underwent title and abstract screening. Of these, 412 full-text articles were assessed for eligibility. Following quality assessment using the Newcastle-Ottawa Scale adapted for AI studies, 127 studies were included in the final qualitative synthesis. The PRISMA flow diagram is presented in Figure 1.

2.5 Risk of Bias Assessment

Each included study was evaluated for risk of bias across seven domains: (1) dataset representativeness, (2) external validation, (3) class imbalance handling, (4) skin tone diversity reporting, (5) model calibration assessment, (6) clinical applicability discussion, and (7) code and data availability. Studies scoring low (less than or equal to 3/7) were flagged for high risk of bias and interpreted with caution.

3. Critical Literature Review

3.1 Evolution of AI Techniques in Dermatology

The application of AI in dermatology has evolved through three distinct phases. Phase I (2012-2017) was characterized by the adoption of standard CNN architectures (AlexNet, VGG, ResNet) for binary and multi-class skin lesion classification. Phase II (2018-2022) saw the introduction of compound-scaled architectures (EfficientNet), attention mechanisms, and the first applications of Vision Transformers. Phase III (2023-present) is defined by multimodal fusion, self-supervised learning, generative AI for data augmentation, and foundation models.

3.2 Convolutional Neural Networks: From ResNet to EfficientNet

CNNs remain the dominant architecture in dermatological AI. ResNet-50 and its variants have been extensively employed as backbone feature extractors, with Tschandl et al. demonstrating expert-level diagnosis of nonpigmented skin cancers using ensemble CNN architectures achieving 88.9% accuracy on challenging diagnostic tasks [12]. DenseNet-201 has shown particular promise in skin lesion classification due to its dense connectivity pattern that encourages feature reuse [13].

EfficientNet architectures, introduced by Tan and Le in 2019, have gained significant traction in dermatology due to their compound scaling methodology that uniformly scales network depth, width, and resolution [14]. Manole et al. (2024) proposed a custom EfficientNetB3-based approach for skin lesion classification achieving 95.4% validation accuracy on four well-represented categories (melanoma, BCC, benign keratosis, melanocytic nevi), though accuracy dropped to 88.8% when including underrepresented classes (SCC, actinic keratosis) [15]. This performance degradation highlights the critical impact of class imbalance on model generalization. Comparative studies by Ali et al. found that intermediate complexity EfficientNet variants (B4, B5) performed optimally on HAM10000, with EfficientNetB4 achieving 87.91% top-1 accuracy [16].

Harahap et al. (2024) investigated all eight EfficientNet variants (B0-B7) on ISIC 2019, with EfficientNetB4 achieving the highest accuracy of 79.69% for BCC, SCC, and melanoma classification [17]. The ensemble approach combining ResNet50, Xception, and EfficientNetB0 with metadata integration achieved 93.2% accuracy and 97.3% AUC on ISIC 2018, demonstrating the substantial benefit of multimodal fusion [18].

3.3 Vision Transformers and Hierarchical Variants

Vision Transformers (ViTs) have emerged as powerful alternatives to CNNs for dermatological image analysis. Unlike CNNs, which rely on local receptive fields, ViTs employ self-attention mechanisms to capture global contextual relationships across the entire image. Wickramarathne and Kumarapathirage introduced DermViT, a vision transformer-based approach achieving 92.48% accuracy on a 4-class skin disease dataset, though limited by sparse explainability features and computational demands [19].

Swin Transformers, which employ shifted window attention mechanisms and hierarchical structures, have shown particular promise. Karthik et al. (2024) proposed a hybrid approach combining Swin Transformer with dense group shuffle non-local attention networks for skin cancer classification [20]. Pacal et al. (2024) enhanced Swin Transformer with hybrid shifted window-based multi-head self-attention and SwiGLU-based MLP for skin cancer diagnosis, demonstrating improved feature extraction capabilities [21]. However, these architectures require substantial computational resources, with quadratic complexity in attention score calculations posing significant barriers to deployment on resource-constrained devices [22].

Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) has been proposed to reduce the computational burden of Swin Transformers by incorporating low-rank matrices into attention weights, enabling efficient fine-tuning without sacrificing performance [23]. This parameter-efficient approach may address the deployment challenges of transformer architectures in clinical settings.

3.4 Hybrid CNN-Transformer Architectures

Hybrid architectures that combine the local feature extraction strengths of CNNs with the global modeling capabilities of Transformers have gained prominence. A Deep CNN Transformer Hybrid Model employing ResNet-50 for local feature extraction followed by ViT for global semantic modeling achieved significantly improved performance on ISIC 2018, particularly when combined with focal loss to address class imbalance [24].

Recent advances include KAN-based fusion approaches that integrate CNN-extracted spatial features with transformer-encoded global dependencies [25]. The proposed CSSL-ETL framework combines ConvNeXt-Large, Swin Transformer V2, and EfficientNetV2 for multi-scale feature extraction, achieving 94.6% accuracy on combined ISIC and HAM10000 datasets-representing a 3.4% improvement over standalone Swin Transformer [26].

3.5 Self-Supervised and Contrastive Learning

Self-supervised learning (SSL) addresses the critical limitation of labeled data scarcity in dermatology. Contrastive learning approaches, particularly SimCLR and Barlow Twins, have been applied to pretrain networks on unlabeled dermoscopic images before fine-tuning on limited labeled data. The CSSL-ETL framework demonstrated that contrastive self-supervised pretraining boosted accuracy from 88.3% (ResNet-50 baseline) to 91.1% [26].

Fu et al. (2024) proposed a Self-Supervision Distribution Calibration Network (SS-DCN) for few-shot rare skin disease classification, integrating rotation prediction and contrastive learning within a multi-task framework [27]. This approach addresses the long-tailed distribution problem inherent in dermatological datasets, where rare diseases are severely underrepresented.

Wang et al. (2024) demonstrated that transfer contrastive learning for Raman spectroscopy skin cancer tissue classification achieved superior feature discrimination compared to standard supervised approaches [28]. The integration of federated self-supervised contrastive learning with masked autoencoders has shown promise for privacy-preserving dermatological disease diagnosis across distributed institutions [29].

3.6 Generative AI and Diffusion Models for Data Augmentation

Generative AI, particularly diffusion models, has emerged as a powerful solution for addressing data scarcity in dermatological AI. Unlike GANs, which suffer from training instability and mode collapse, diffusion models demonstrate superior stability and diversity in medical image generation [30].

A recent study enhanced the Stable Diffusion model with 8-channel VAE and multi-level CLIP embeddings for skin disease data augmentation, improving classification accuracy from 80.92% to 84.15% when combining synthetic and real data [31]. The approach employed lesion area masks and latent space interpolation to preserve fine-grained details critical for diagnostic accuracy. However, limitations remain in generating completely realistic normal skin regions and ensuring clinical validity of synthetic images.

Derm-T2IM developed a text-to-image skin lesion synthesis model based on Stable Diffusion, demonstrating that stable model tuning is possible with only 1,400 images per class [32]. SkinDiff proposed a novel latent diffusion model approach for skin lesion segmentation, while BADM (Boundary-Assisted Diffusion Model) improved segmentation through diffusion-based augmentation [33].

3.7 Explainable AI in Dermatology

The black box nature of deep learning models poses significant barriers to clinical adoption. Explainable AI (XAI) methods have been extensively applied to dermatological AI to provide visual and textual explanations for model predictions.

SkinSage XAI utilized Inception v3 with Grad-CAM and LIME algorithms, achieving 96% accuracy on customized HAM10000 while providing transparent visual explanations for seven skin lesion categories [34]. The multimodal explainable AI framework integrating 3D Total Body Photography (TBP) with clinical parameters employed SHAP and Class Activation Mapping (CAM) to identify key predictors corresponding to established dermatological diagnostic criteria including asymmetry, border irregularity, and pigment variation [8].

Gwilliam et al. (2023) combined ResNet-50 with XAI techniques, achieving 89.5% accuracy with full explainability coverage, compared to DermViT's limited XAI capabilities [35]. The proposed EfficientNetV2-L + Attention approach achieved 91.15% accuracy with comprehensive Grad-CAM and saliency map explanations [19].

Despite these advances, significant limitations persist. Most XAI methods provide post-hoc explanations that may not faithfully represent the model's actual decision-making process. The clinical utility of saliency maps remains debated, as dermatologists require explanations aligned with established diagnostic criteria (ABCDE rule, 7-point checklist) rather than pixel-level attention maps.

3.8 Federated Learning and Privacy-Preserving AI

Federated learning (FL) enables model training across multiple institutions without centralizing sensitive patient data, addressing critical privacy concerns in dermatological AI. The CSSL-ETL framework integrated federated learning for multi-center deployment, demonstrating that privacy-preserving training can achieve comparable accuracy to centralized approaches [26].

Riaz et al. (2023) conducted a prospective study comparing federated and transfer learning methods for melanoma and non-melanoma skin cancer classification, demonstrating the feasibility of privacy-preserving collaborative learning [36]. Wu et al. (2022) proposed federated self-supervised contrastive learning with masked autoencoders for dermatological disease diagnosis, achieving 90.4% accuracy on private dermatology datasets [29].

However, federated learning in dermatology faces unique challenges including non-IID (independent and identically distributed) data distributions across institutions, heterogeneous imaging equipment, and varying annotation standards. The lack of standardized federated learning benchmarks for dermatology limits reproducibility and comparative evaluation.

3.9 Multimodal Learning and Clinical Decision Support

Multimodal AI systems that integrate imaging data with clinical metadata have demonstrated superior diagnostic performance compared to unimodal approaches. The explainable multimodal AI framework fusing 3D Total Body Photography (TBP) with structured clinical parameters achieved AUC values exceeding 0.95 across all six lesion categories, with peak AUC of 0.98 for nevus and actinic keratosis [8]. In contrast, unimodal models showed lower accuracy (72.6-87.1%).

The C4C risk factor framework identified seven primary risk factors (lesion pinkness, size, color, inflammation, shape, age, and natural hair color) pertinent to all three main skin cancer types. Fusing these factors with 11 external risk factors achieved sensitivity of 85.24% and specificity of 61.12% for suspicious skin lesion detection [37].

MediFact at MEDIQA-M3G 2024 proposed a weakly supervised multimodal approach for open-ended dermatological question answering, leveraging VGG16-CNN-SVM models and ViT-CLIP for multilingual (English, Chinese, Spanish) skin condition representation learning [38].

3.10 Foundation Models and Large Vision Models

Foundation models pretrained on massive datasets and fine-tuned for downstream tasks represent a paradigm shift in medical AI. PanDerm, a multimodal vision foundation model for clinical dermatology, achieved 85.38% accuracy and 85.38% weighted F1 on HAM10000 through fine-tuning, significantly outperforming BioMedGPT (73.94% accuracy) [39]. The model was pretrained on diverse dermatological images using contrastive learning with 4x80GB NVIDIA H100 GPUs, demonstrating the computational requirements of foundation model development.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) frameworks have been proposed to enhance diagnostic accuracy by grounding LLM outputs in peer-reviewed dermatological literature. A biomedical literature Q&A system using RAG achieved 57% exact match accuracy with fine-tuned Mistral-7B, while ChatCAD+ integrated hierarchical in-context learning for image-based diagnostic report generation [40].

3.11 Teledermatology and AI-Enabled Medical Devices

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of teledermatology, creating new opportunities for AI integration. A prospective trial evaluating an AI as a Medical Device (AIaMD) in a UK teledermatology service demonstrated that the AIaMD had a significantly higher rate of identifying lesions that did not need biopsy or urgent referral compared to standard of care (p=0.001), with comparable sensitivity for skin cancer (91.0% vs. 97.0%) [41].

Systematic reviews of teledermatology report diagnostic accuracy rates of approximately 75-80% compared to in-person clinical care, with sensitivity around 95% and specificity around 84% for malignant skin lesions using photographic images [42]. However, smartphone applications with algorithm-based software have shown lower reliability compared to consultant dermatologist assessment.

DermaSensor, an FDA-cleared and CE-marked AI-enabled device using spectroscopy for primary care skin cancer detection, represents a milestone in regulatory approval of AI dermatology tools [9]. The FDA's health equity-focused regulatory approach ensures diverse patient populations are tracked through post-market surveillance, potentially shaping future regulatory standards for AI-enabled medical devices.

4.2 Critical Analysis of Dataset Limitations

4.2.1 Skin Tone and Ethnic Diversity Crisis

Perhaps the most critical gap in dermatological AI datasets is the profound underrepresentation of darker skin tones. A comprehensive review of 54 skin lesion datasets found that only three (PAD-UFES-20, Fitzpatrick17k, and DDI) provide Fitzpatrick skin type metadata [52]. Among the 16,012 labeled images in Fitzpatrick17k, only 635 (3.97%) represent Fitzpatrick skin type VI, while PAD-UFES-20 contains merely one image (0.07%) of Fitzpatrick VI [52].

This underrepresentation is not merely a statistical concern-it has direct clinical consequences. AI models trained on predominantly light-skinned datasets demonstrate significantly reduced accuracy when applied to darker skin tones, potentially exacerbating existing healthcare disparities. The DDI dataset, while balanced across three aggregated skin type groups, is too small (656 images) to train generalizable deep learning models for all skin types [52].

4.2.2 Class Imbalance and Label Noise

Class imbalance is endemic in dermatological datasets. In HAM10000, the melanocytic nevus (NV) class comprises 6,705 images while dermatofibroma (DF) contains only 115 images-a ratio of approximately 58:1 [43]. BCN20000 exhibits a similar imbalance ratio of approximately 40:1 [45]. This imbalance causes models to develop severe bias toward majority classes, compromising melanoma detection sensitivity-the most clinically critical metric.

Label noise presents an additional challenge. In HAM10000, 46.7% of diagnoses are based on clinical consensus rather than histopathological confirmation, introducing potential mislabeling [43]. The ISIC archive's multi-source nature compounds this issue, with images collected from diverse institutions using varying equipment, protocols, and annotation standards.

4.2.3 Domain Shift and Generalization

Domain shift-the performance degradation when models are deployed on data from different distributions than their training data-represents a fundamental barrier to clinical deployment. Dermoscopic images differ systematically from clinical photographs in terms of lighting, magnification, and background. Models trained on HAM10000 (dermoscopy) typically show 15-30% accuracy drops when evaluated on PAD-UFES-20 (clinical smartphone images) [45].

The BCN20000 dataset specifically addresses this gap by including lesions in the wild-unconstrained dermoscopic images including hard-to-diagnose locations (nails, mucosa), large lesions, and hypo-pigmented lesions [49]. However, even this comprehensive dataset lacks skin tone metadata, limiting its utility for fairness analysis.

5.3 CNN vs. Transformer: Critical Analysis

The choice between CNN and Transformer architectures for dermatological AI involves trade-offs across multiple dimensions:

Inductive Bias: CNNs possess strong inductive biases for spatial locality and translation equivariance, which align well with dermatological diagnostic patterns (ABCDE criteria). Transformers lack these biases but can learn them from sufficient data, requiring larger datasets for comparable performance.

Computational Efficiency: CNNs generally offer superior inference efficiency, with EfficientNet-B3 requiring approximately 1.8B FLOPs compared to 17.6B FLOPs for ViT-Base. This efficiency gap is critical for mobile teledermatology applications and point-of-care deployment.

Long-Range Dependencies: Transformers excel at modeling global contextual relationships, which is particularly relevant for lesions distributed across the entire image rather than localized to specific regions [22]. However, the clinical relevance of these global dependencies for dermatological diagnosis remains underexplored.

Interpretability: CNNs offer more straightforward interpretability through gradient-based attention maps, while Transformer attention heads provide complex multi-head attention patterns that may be less intuitive for clinicians.

6. Major Research Gaps: Critical Analysis

This section identifies and critically discusses 25 major research gaps categorized into six thematic areas. Each gap is classified as either explicitly reported (directly acknowledged in primary literature) or inferred (identified through systematic analysis but not explicitly discussed by original authors).

6.1 Dataset and Data Quality Gaps

Gap 1: Severe Skin Tone Underrepresentation (Explicitly Reported)

Fitzpatrick types V-VI constitute less than 4% of images in the largest labeled dataset (Fitzpatrick17k) and only 0.07% in PAD-UFES-20 [52]. This gap directly threatens equitable AI deployment and may exacerbate healthcare disparities for darker-skinned populations.

Gap 2: Extreme Class Imbalance (Explicitly Reported)

Imbalance ratios exceeding 58:1 in HAM10000 and 40:1 in BCN20000 cause models to develop severe bias toward majority classes, compromising melanoma detection sensitivity-the most clinically critical metric [43] [45].

Gap 3: Label Noise and Diagnostic Uncertainty (Inferred)

Up to 46.7% of HAM10000 labels rely on clinical consensus rather than histopathological gold standard. No published study has quantified the impact of label noise on model calibration or fairness metrics.

Gap 4: Limited Rare Disease Coverage (Explicitly Reported)

Over 6,000 rare skin diseases affect approximately 7% of the global population, yet SD-198 (the largest rare disease dataset) contains only 198 classes with severe long-tail distribution [48] [27].

Gap 5: Absence of Standardized Skin Tone Annotation (Explicitly Reported)

Only 3 of 54 identified datasets provide Fitzpatrick skin type metadata. The lack of standardized annotation protocols prevents systematic fairness auditing across the field [52].

6.2 Model Architecture and Performance Gaps

Gap 6: Poor Model Calibration (Inferred)

Despite achieving high accuracy, most dermatological AI models exhibit poor calibration-predicted probabilities do not reflect true likelihoods. This gap is critical for clinical decision-making where probability thresholds guide biopsy decisions.

Gap 7: Weak Uncertainty Quantification (Inferred)

Only a minority of studies employ Bayesian methods or Monte Carlo dropout for uncertainty estimation. The SkiNet pipeline integrated Bayesian MultiResUNet and Bayesian DenseNet-169 but achieved only 73.65% accuracy [53], suggesting a trade-off between uncertainty quantification and accuracy.

Gap 8: Limited Continual Learning Capability (Explicitly Reported)

Catastrophic forgetting remains a significant problem when models are updated with new disease classes or data distributions. The wake-sleep consolidated learning (WSCL) approach has shown promise in reducing forgetting but has not been evaluated on dermatological tasks [54].

Gap 9: High Computational Cost and Green AI Challenges (Inferred)

Foundation models like PanDerm require 4x80GB H100 GPUs for pretraining [39]. The environmental and economic costs of training such models are rarely discussed, particularly for deployment in resource-limited settings.

6.3 Explainability and Clinical Trust Gaps

Gap 10: Misalignment Between XAI Outputs and Clinical Reasoning (Explicitly Reported)

While Grad-CAM and SHAP provide visual explanations, these rarely align with established dermatological diagnostic criteria (ABCDE rule, 7-point checklist, pattern analysis). Clinicians require explanations grounded in medical knowledge, not pixel-level attention maps [8].

Gap 11: Lack of Causal Explanation Methods (Inferred)

No published dermatological AI study employs causal inference methods to explain why specific features cause particular diagnoses. Current XAI methods are correlational, limiting their utility for clinical education and model improvement.

Gap 12: Insufficient Human-AI Interaction Studies (Inferred)

The impact of AI explanations on clinician diagnostic accuracy, confidence, and cognitive load remains largely unstudied. Most XAI evaluations use proxy metrics (localization accuracy) rather than clinical utility measures.

6.4 Validation and Generalization Gaps

Gap 13: Lack of External Validation (Explicitly Reported)

Our analysis indicates that 78% of published dermatological AI studies evaluate models only on held-out test sets from the same distribution as training data. External validation on geographically, demographically, and technically distinct datasets is critically lacking.

Gap 14: Weak Clinical Validation (Explicitly Reported)

Prospective clinical trials are absent in 94% of AI dermatology devices. The Dermalyser trial for melanoma thickness assessment and the UK AIaMD teledermatology study represent rare exceptions [10] [41].

Gap 15: Domain Shift Ignorance (Inferred)

Most studies fail to evaluate performance degradation across dermoscopy-to-clinical, smartphone-to-clinical, and cross-population domain shifts. The BCN20000 lesions in the wild approach addresses this partially but lacks skin tone metadata [49].

6.5 Fairness and Equity Gaps

Gap 16: Skin Tone Bias (Explicitly Reported)

Models trained on predominantly light-skinned datasets demonstrate significantly reduced sensitivity for melanoma detection in darker skin tones. This bias has been documented in facial recognition but remains underexplored in dermatological AI [52].

Gap 17: Geographic and Ethnic Bias (Inferred)

Major datasets are predominantly sourced from European and North American populations (HAM10000: Austria; BCN20000: Spain; PAD-UFES-20: Brazil European descendants). The lack of African, South Asian, and Indigenous population representation limits global applicability.

Gap 18: Age and Gender Bias (Inferred)

Most datasets skew toward older populations (PAD-UFES-20 median age ~60 years) with limited pediatric representation. Gender distribution is rarely balanced or reported as a stratification variable in performance analysis.

6.6 Deployment and Regulatory Gaps

Gap 19: Lack of Regulatory Compliance Frameworks (Explicitly Reported)

Only a handful of AI dermatology devices have achieved FDA clearance or CE marking. The DermaSensor authorization represents a milestone, but regulatory pathways for AI dermatology remain unclear for most applications [9].

Gap 20: Insufficient Deployment Studies (Inferred)

Most published studies stop at algorithm development without addressing real-world deployment challenges including latency, bandwidth requirements, device compatibility, user interface design, and integration with electronic health records.

Gap 21: Privacy and Security Concerns (Explicitly Reported)

While federated learning addresses data centralization concerns, differential privacy guarantees, secure aggregation, and adversarial robustness are rarely implemented or evaluated in dermatological FL systems [26].

Gap 22: Weak Reproducibility (Inferred)

Code and data availability remains poor across the field. Only a minority of studies provide open-source implementations, and hyperparameter configurations are often incompletely reported, preventing independent replication.

Gap 23: Limited Multimodal Learning (Explicitly Reported)

While multimodal approaches show promise, most studies treat metadata as auxiliary features rather than integrating imaging, clinical history, genomics, and patient-reported outcomes into unified diagnostic frameworks [8].

Gap 24: Poor Benchmarking Standards (Inferred)

The lack of standardized benchmarking protocols (fixed train/val/test splits, consistent evaluation metrics, mandatory external validation) prevents meaningful comparison across studies. The ISIC challenge provides partial standardization but evolves annually.

Gap 25: Agentic AI Absence (Inferred)

While RAG frameworks have been proposed for medical diagnosis, no dermatological AI system incorporates autonomous agentic capabilities for iterative diagnostic reasoning, differential diagnosis generation, or treatment recommendation based on evolving patient data.

7. Quantitative Analysis

7.1 Publication Trends

Analysis of the 127 included studies reveals exponential growth in AI dermatology publications, with a 340% increase from 2020 (n=18) to 2025 (n=61). Vision Transformer-based studies increased from 2 (2020) to 28 (2025), while federated learning publications grew from 1 to 12 in the same period.

7.2 Dataset Usage Statistics

Among the included studies, HAM10000 was the most frequently used dataset (68 studies, 53.5%), followed by ISIC Archive (54 studies, 42.5%), and PAD-UFES-20 (23 studies, 18.1%). Only 8 studies (6.3%) employed Fitzpatrick17k or DDI for skin tone analysis.

7.3 Model Popularity

EfficientNet variants were the most popular CNN architecture (45 studies, 35.4%), followed by ResNet (38 studies, 29.9%), and DenseNet (21 studies, 16.5%). Among Transformers, Swin Transformer was most common (19 studies), followed by vanilla ViT (14 studies) and DermViT (6 studies).

7.4 Clinical Validation Statistics

Only 7 studies (5.5%) reported prospective clinical validation. External validation on independent datasets was reported in 28 studies (22.0%). Skin tone stratified performance analysis was reported in just 4 studies (3.1%). Model calibration assessment (ECE, Brier score) was reported in 6 studies (4.7%).

8. Challenges and Future Research Directions

Based on the identified research gaps, we propose 25 novel future research directions organized by thematic area. Each direction includes recommended datasets, evaluation metrics, and architectural approaches.

8.1 Dataset and Data Quality Directions

Direction 1: Diverse Skin Tone Dataset Construction
Objective: Create a large-scale dermatological dataset with balanced Fitzpatrick I-VI representation (greater than 15,000 images, greater than 15% per type).
Dataset: Combine Fitzpatrick17k, DDI, PAD-UFES-20 with new collections from African, South Asian, and Indigenous populations.
Metrics: Demographic parity, equalized odds, calibration across skin types.
Architecture: Domain-adversarial neural networks with fairness constraints.
Direction 2: Synthetic Data Generation with Clinical Validity
Objective: Develop diffusion models that generate clinically valid synthetic lesions with pathology-confirmed fidelity.
Dataset: ISIC + BCN20000 + paired histopathology.
Metrics: FID, IS, clinician validation score, downstream classification improvement.
Architecture: Latent diffusion models with pathology-conditioned generation.
Direction 3: Long-Tail Rare Disease Learning
Objective: Develop few-shot and zero-shot learning methods for greater than 500 rare skin diseases.
Dataset: SD-198 + Derm7pt + Orphanet clinical descriptions.
Metrics: 5-way 1-shot accuracy, generalization to unseen classes.
Architecture: Prototypical networks with self-supervised pretraining + text-image contrastive learning.
Direction 4: Label Noise Robust Learning
Objective: Develop training strategies robust to 20-50% label noise in dermatological datasets.
Dataset: HAM10000 with noise injection simulation.
Metrics: Accuracy under noise, noise detection precision/recall.
Architecture: Co-teaching + mixup + confident learning.

8.2 Model Architecture Directions

Direction 5: Efficient Hybrid CNN-Transformer Architectures
Objective: Design architectures combining CNN efficiency with Transformer global modeling at less than 5M parameters.
Dataset: HAM10000 + ISIC 2024.
Metrics: Accuracy, FLOPs, latency on mobile CPU.
Architecture: Mobile-friendly Swin with depthwise separable convolutions + token pruning.
Direction 6: Calibrated Uncertainty-Aware Models
Objective: Develop dermatological AI with well-calibrated uncertainty estimates suitable for clinical triage.
Dataset: ISIC + PAD-UFES-20.
Metrics: ECE, Brier score, NLL, rejection accuracy curve.
Architecture: Deep ensembles + temperature scaling + Bayesian neural networks.
Direction 7: Continual Learning for Evolving Disease Taxonomy
Objective: Enable models to learn new disease classes without forgetting existing knowledge.
Dataset: Sequential ISIC challenges (2016-2024).
Metrics: Average accuracy, backward transfer, forward transfer.
Architecture: Elastic Weight Consolidation + replay buffers + WSCL-inspired dreaming phases.
Direction 8: Green AI for Resource-Limited Settings
Objective: Develop accurate dermatological AI models trainable on single GPUs and deployable on smartphones.
Dataset: PAD-UFES-20 + custom smartphone clinical dataset.
Metrics: Accuracy, model size (less than 10MB), inference time (less than 100ms), energy consumption.
Architecture: Neural architecture search with mobile constraints + knowledge distillation.

8.3 Explainability and Clinical Trust Directions

Direction 9: Clinically Aligned XAI
Objective: Develop explanation methods that map model decisions to established dermatological criteria (ABCDE, 7-point checklist, pattern analysis).
Dataset: HAM10000 with structured ABCDE annotations.
Metrics: Clinician explanation satisfaction, diagnostic concordance with ABCDE scoring.
Architecture: Concept bottleneck models + neuro-symbolic integration.
Direction 10: Causal Explainability
Objective: Employ causal inference to identify true causal features rather than spurious correlations.
Dataset: ISIC with controlled confounding variables.
Metrics: Causal effect estimates, interventional accuracy.
Architecture: Causal VAE + structural causal models.
Direction 11: Human-AI Collaborative Diagnosis
Objective: Design interactive systems that improve clinician diagnostic accuracy while reducing cognitive load.
Dataset: Prospective clinical trial with dermatologist-AI interaction logging.
Metrics: Diagnostic accuracy improvement, time-to-diagnosis, clinician trust score.
Architecture: Adaptive explanation generation + active learning interface.

8.4 Validation and Generalization Directions

Direction 12: Multi-Center External Validation Framework
Objective: Establish standardized protocols for external validation across diverse clinical settings.
Dataset: Federated collection from 10+ institutions across 5 continents.
Metrics: Accuracy, sensitivity, specificity stratified by geography, device, skin tone.
Architecture: Federated learning with domain generalization techniques.
Direction 13: Prospective Clinical Trials for AI Dermatology
Objective: Conduct RCTs comparing AI-assisted diagnosis to standard of care in real clinical workflows.
Dataset: Prospective multi-center trial (n greater than 5,000 lesions).
Metrics: Sensitivity, specificity, NNB, time-to-diagnosis, patient outcomes.
Architecture: Regulatory-compliant AIaMD with audit trails.
Direction 14: Domain Generalization Across Imaging Modalities
Objective: Develop models that generalize across dermoscopy, clinical photography, smartphone images, and TBP.
Dataset: HAM10000 + PAD-UFES-20 + BCN20000 + TBP datasets.
Metrics: Cross-domain accuracy, domain adaptation performance.
Architecture: Domain-invariant feature learning + meta-learning.

8.5 Fairness and Equity Directions

Direction 15: Skin Tone-Aware Training
Objective: Develop training strategies that explicitly optimize for equitable performance across all Fitzpatrick types.
Dataset: Fitzpatrick17k + DDI + new balanced collections.
Metrics: Equalized odds, demographic parity, calibration by skin type.
Architecture: Fairness-constrained optimization + reweighting + adversarial debiasing.
Direction 16: Geographic Generalization
Objective: Ensure models perform equitably across populations from different geographic and ethnic backgrounds.
Dataset: Multi-ethnic dataset from Africa, Asia, South America, Indigenous communities.
Metrics: Accuracy stratified by ethnicity, geographic origin, socioeconomic status.
Architecture: Federated learning with fairness constraints + invariant risk minimization.
Direction 17: Pediatric Dermatology AI
Objective: Develop specialized models for pediatric skin conditions with age-appropriate feature learning.
Dataset: Pediatric dermatology atlas + SD-198 pediatric subset.
Metrics: Age-stratified accuracy, developmental appropriateness.
Architecture: Age-conditioned neural networks + curriculum learning.

8.6 Deployment and Regulatory Directions

Direction 18: Regulatory-Compliant AI Pipelines
Objective: Develop end-to-end AI development pipelines that satisfy FDA/CE regulatory requirements from inception.
Dataset: Any benchmark dataset with full provenance documentation.
Metrics: Regulatory checklist compliance, audit trail completeness.
Architecture: MLOps with built-in documentation, version control, and explainability logging.
Direction 19: Secure Federated Learning for Dermatology
Objective: Implement differential privacy, secure aggregation, and Byzantine-robust aggregation in dermatological FL.
Dataset: Simulated multi-institutional dermatology data.
Metrics: Privacy budget (epsilon), model accuracy under attacks, convergence rate.
Architecture: Differentially private SGD + secure multi-party computation.
Direction 20: Real-World Deployment Studies
Objective: Conduct comprehensive deployment studies measuring latency, usability, integration, and clinical impact.
Dataset: Real-world clinical deployment with EHR integration.
Metrics: System uptime, user satisfaction, diagnostic workflow efficiency, cost-effectiveness.
Architecture: Edge-cloud hybrid deployment with offline capability.

8.7 Emerging Paradigm Directions

Direction 21: Foundation Models for Dermatology
Objective: Develop dermatology-specific foundation models pretrained on diverse skin images with clinical metadata.
Dataset: All public dermatology datasets + clinical text corpora.
Metrics: Zero-shot transfer, few-shot adaptation, cross-task generalization.
Architecture: Large vision-language models with dermatology-specific pretraining objectives.
Direction 22: Retrieval-Augmented Diagnostic Systems
Objective: Build RAG-based systems that ground diagnostic recommendations in peer-reviewed dermatological literature.
Dataset: PubMed dermatology corpus + ISIC images + clinical guidelines.
Metrics: Citation accuracy, diagnostic concordance with guidelines, hallucination rate.
Architecture: Multimodal RAG with vision encoder + LLM + vector database.
Direction 23: Agentic AI for Dermatology
Objective: Develop autonomous diagnostic agents capable of iterative reasoning, differential diagnosis, and treatment planning.
Dataset: Comprehensive dermatology knowledge base + patient case simulations.
Metrics: Diagnostic accuracy chain, treatment recommendation appropriateness, safety checks.
Architecture: Multi-agent systems with dermatology expert models + safety guardrails.
Direction 24: Graph Neural Networks for Skin Lesion Analysis
Objective: Model spatial relationships between skin regions and lesion characteristics using graph structures.
Dataset: TBP datasets with spatial lesion mapping + clinical networks.
Metrics: Graph classification accuracy, lesion relationship prediction.
Architecture: Graph attention networks + spatial graph convolutional networks.
Direction 25: Standardized Benchmarking Initiative
Objective: Establish community-wide benchmarking standards with fixed splits, mandatory external validation, and fairness reporting.
Dataset: Curated multi-source dataset with standardized annotations.
Metrics: Leaderboard rankings with confidence intervals, reproducibility scores.
Architecture: Benchmark-agnostic evaluation framework with automated reporting.

9. Discussion

9.1 Synthesis of Findings

This systematic review reveals a field in transition-from algorithmic innovation toward clinical translation. While technical performance on benchmark datasets has reached impressive levels, the pathway from laboratory accuracy to clinical utility remains obstructed by fundamental gaps in dataset diversity, model reliability, and regulatory readiness.

The most concerning finding is the profound underrepresentation of darker skin tones across all major datasets. With Fitzpatrick types V-VI comprising less than 4% of labeled images, current AI dermatology systems risk perpetuating and amplifying existing healthcare disparities. This is not merely an academic concern; melanoma mortality rates are already higher among Black and Hispanic populations in the United States, and biased AI systems could further delay diagnosis in these communities.

The class imbalance problem, while widely acknowledged, remains inadequately addressed. Most studies employ standard oversampling or focal loss techniques without considering the clinical cost of false negatives versus false positives. In melanoma detection, a false negative (missed melanoma) carries vastly greater clinical cost than a false positive (unnecessary biopsy), yet most evaluation metrics treat these errors symmetrically.

9.2 Implications for Clinical Practice

For clinicians considering AI integration, several practical implications emerge. First, current AI tools should be viewed as decision support rather than autonomous diagnostic systems. The lack of prospective clinical validation means that real-world performance may differ substantially from reported benchmark accuracies. Second, clinicians should be aware of demographic bias in training data and exercise heightened vigilance when evaluating lesions in underrepresented populations. Third, the interpretability gap means that AI explanations should supplement, not replace, clinical judgment.

9.3 Implications for Researchers

For researchers, this review highlights the urgent need for methodological rigor. Every future study should report: (1) skin tone stratified performance, (2) external validation on independent datasets, (3) model calibration metrics, (4) uncertainty quantification, and (5) code availability. The field would benefit enormously from a standardized benchmarking protocol analogous to ImageNet for general computer vision.

9.4 Implications for Policymakers and Regulators

The FDA's health equity-focused approach to DermaSensor authorization represents a promising regulatory direction [9]. Future regulatory frameworks should mandate: (1) demographic performance reporting, (2) prospective clinical trials for high-risk applications, (3) post-market surveillance for bias detection, and (4) transparency requirements for training data composition. The EU AI Act's risk-based classification of medical AI provides an additional regulatory template that could be adapted for dermatological applications.

9.5 Limitations of This Review

This review has several limitations. First, the search was limited to English-language publications, potentially excluding relevant research from non-English speaking regions. Second, the quantitative statistics (e.g., 78% lack external validation) are estimates based on the included literature and may not generalize to the entire field. Third, the rapid pace of publication means that some recent advances may not be fully captured. Fourth, the proposed future directions represent informed recommendations rather than empirically validated pathways.

10. Conclusion

AI-based dermatology has achieved remarkable technical progress over the past five years, with deep learning models demonstrating expert-level accuracy on benchmark datasets and regulatory authorities beginning to clear AI-enabled devices for clinical use. However, this systematic review reveals that the field remains in a pre-clinical phase for most applications, with 25 critical research gaps spanning dataset diversity, model reliability, clinical validation, fairness, and deployment readiness.

The most urgent priorities are: (1) constructing diverse, representative datasets that include adequate representation of all skin tones, ages, and ethnicities; (2) developing calibrated, uncertainty-aware models that clinicians can trust; (3) conducting rigorous prospective clinical trials to establish real-world efficacy; (4) creating explainable AI methods aligned with clinical reasoning; and (5) building regulatory-compliant, privacy-preserving deployment pipelines.

The 25 future research directions proposed in this review provide a roadmap for addressing these gaps. Success will require unprecedented collaboration between computer scientists, dermatologists, ethicists, regulators, and patient advocates. Only through such multidisciplinary efforts can AI dermatology fulfill its transformative potential while ensuring equitable, safe, and effective care for all patients regardless of skin tone, geography, or socioeconomic status.

The next decade will determine whether AI dermatology becomes a tool that narrows healthcare disparities or one that widens them. The choices made by researchers, clinicians, and policymakers today will shape this trajectory for generations to come.
